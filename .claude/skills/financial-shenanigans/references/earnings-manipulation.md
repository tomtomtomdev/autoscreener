# Earnings Manipulation (EM) Shenanigans

The seven techniques management uses to inflate or smooth reported profit. Schilit numbers them EM1–EM7. EM1–EM5 inflate the current period; EM6–EM7 shift income or expenses between periods to manufacture a smoother or better-timed trend.

For each: what it is, how it's done, the detection cues (the numbers that betray it), and a classic case.

## EM1 — Recording revenue too soon

**What it is:** Booking revenue before the earnings process is complete — before delivery, before the customer is obligated, or before services are performed.

**How it's done:**
- Recognizing revenue on signed contracts before shipment or before the customer accepts.
- Recognizing the full value of multi-period deals (subscriptions, licenses, maintenance) up front instead of over the service period.
- Recognizing revenue when significant obligations to the customer remain (installation, future services, right of return).
- Aggressive percentage-of-completion estimates on long-term contracts.
- Recognizing revenue on shipments to a reseller/distributor before there's real end demand ("channel stuffing" — overlaps EM2).

**Detection cues:**
- **DSO climbing** — receivables growing faster than revenue. The classic signature.
- A jump in **unbilled receivables** (revenue recognized but not yet invoiced) — common in long-term-contract abuse.
- **Deferred revenue falling** while reported revenue rises (revenue being pulled out of the deferred bucket too fast).
- Revenue-recognition policy changes, or new language about "delivery terms" or "acceptance," in the footnotes.
- A surge in revenue concentrated in the last days of the quarter.

**Case:** Computer Associates booked license revenue on contracts before they were finalized, even backdating quarters. Sunbeam's "bill-and-hold" sales (booking revenue for goods kept in Sunbeam's own warehouses).

## EM2 — Recording bogus revenue

**What it is:** Revenue that isn't real revenue at all — fake, or arising from transactions that lack economic substance.

**How it's done:**
- Recording revenue from transactions that lack a genuine customer or lack economic substance.
- **Round-trip / "boomerang" deals** — paying a counterparty who then "buys" from you, so cash circles back as revenue.
- Recording cash received from a lender or supplier as revenue.
- Recognizing revenue from a sale where the company is really acting as an agent (should be net) but books the gross amount.
- Inflating revenue via swaps of similar assets/services at inflated values (capacity swaps among telecoms).
- Recognizing supplier rebates or one-off settlements as revenue.

**Detection cues:**
- Revenue growth wildly out of line with the industry or with unit/volume metrics.
- Related-party transactions disclosed in footnotes; revenue from entities the company also pays.
- A new, vaguely described revenue category appearing suddenly.
- Gross-vs-net presentation that flatters the top line without matching gross profit.
- Cash flow from operations failing to follow the "revenue" up.

**Case:** Enron's round-trip energy/broadband-capacity swaps. Lucent and other telecoms booking fiber-capacity swaps as revenue.

## EM3 — Boosting income using one-time or unsustainable activities

**What it is:** Inflating *income* (often below the revenue line) with gains that are non-recurring or not part of core operations, while presenting them as if they're ordinary.

**How it's done:**
- Recording a one-time gain (asset sale, legal settlement, insurance recovery) inside operating income.
- Releasing reserves into earnings (see EM5/EM7) to plug a shortfall.
- Recording investment income or pension gains as if operational.
- Misclassifying a one-time gain as a reduction of operating expense.
- Booking gains on the sale of a business as recurring revenue/income.

**Detection cues:**
- **Operating margin spikes** that don't match revenue or cost trends.
- "Other income," gains, or settlements buried in operating lines rather than below the line.
- A sudden, unexplained drop in an expense line (a reserve release netted against it).
- Pension/investment income swinging the bottom line for a non-financial company.
- The gap between **GAAP operating income and a clean, recurring measure** widening.

**Case:** Companies plugging earnings shortfalls with asset-sale gains and pension income presented as operating performance.

## EM4 — Shifting current expenses to a later period

**What it is:** Capitalizing or deferring costs that should be expensed now, so current-period profit looks higher (the costs hit later, or never).

**How it's done:**
- **Improperly capitalizing operating costs** as assets (the WorldCom playbook).
- Capitalizing software-development, marketing, or customer-acquisition costs aggressively.
- Depreciating/amortizing too slowly, or extending useful lives, to shrink the periodic expense.
- Failing to write down impaired or obsolete assets.
- Amortizing capitalized costs over an unrealistically long period.

**Detection cues:**
- **Capitalized costs / "other assets" growing faster than revenue.**
- A line like "capitalized software" or "deferred customer-acquisition cost" ballooning.
- Useful-life or depreciation-method changes in the footnotes that lower expense.
- **CFO diverging from net income** — capitalized costs land in *investing* outflows, so operations look cash-rich while income is propped up (overlaps CF2).
- Falling depreciation expense as a % of gross PP&E.

**Case:** WorldCom capitalized ~$11B of ordinary line-cost operating expenses as capital assets — the largest accounting fraud of its era.

## EM5 — Employing other techniques to hide expenses or losses

**What it is:** A grab-bag for hiding costs and losses that don't fit EM4's capitalization pattern.

**How it's done:**
- **Failing to record or under-reserving for expenses/liabilities** — warranty, litigation, bad debt, returns.
- Releasing prior over-reserves to offset current costs ("cookie-jar" reserves — overlaps EM7).
- Using off-balance-sheet entities or operating leases to keep liabilities (and their costs) off the books.
- Recording too low an allowance for doubtful accounts relative to receivables.
- Failing to write down inventory or impaired investments.
- Stretching loss-contingency recognition.

**Detection cues:**
- **Reserve/allowance ratios falling** — allowance for doubtful accounts ÷ gross receivables, warranty reserve ÷ sales, inventory reserve ÷ inventory.
- Liabilities flat or shrinking while the business and its obligations grow.
- Large off-balance-sheet commitments disclosed only in footnotes.
- "Change in estimate" language reducing an accrual right when earnings would otherwise miss.
- A jump in receivables aging without a matching jump in the allowance.

**Case:** Companies maintaining inadequate bad-debt or warranty reserves, then releasing "cookie-jar" reserves to smooth quarters.

## EM6 — Shifting current income to a later period

**What it is:** *Hiding* income now to create a cushion for later — the smoothing direction. Often done in unusually good periods, around acquisitions, or to "save" for a future shortfall.

**How it's done:**
- Creating excess reserves in a good period (or via "big-bath" acquisition accounting) to release later.
- Improperly deferring current revenue into deferred-revenue buckets to be released when needed.
- At acquisition, writing off in-process R&D or over-accruing restructuring/acquisition reserves, then bleeding them back into income.

**Detection cues:**
- A sudden build in **deferred revenue or accruals** in a strong period that lacks an operational reason.
- Large acquisition-related write-offs/charges followed by suspiciously smooth subsequent earnings.
- Reserves that grow in fat years and shrink in lean ones (the smoothing fingerprint).

**Case:** "Big-bath" merger reserves and in-process R&D write-offs (a pattern across 1990s tech acquirers) later released to flatter post-deal earnings.

## EM7 — Shifting future expenses to the current period

**What it is:** Accelerating future costs into the present — usually into a period that's already bad ("big bath") so future periods look better, or to set up releasable reserves.

**How it's done:**
- **"Big bath" restructuring charges** — over-accruing so the reserve can be quietly reversed into future income.
- Writing down assets too aggressively in a bad period to reduce future depreciation.
- Accelerating discretionary spending into a year that's already lost.
- Impairing inventory/assets beyond what's warranted, boosting later gross margins on the sale of those written-down items.

**Detection cues:**
- A large **restructuring or impairment charge**, followed by reversals or unusually high margins in later periods.
- Charges taken in a quarter management has already conceded.
- Write-downs that exceed peer norms for similar conditions.
- The same "non-recurring" charge recurring year after year (serial restructurers).

**Case:** Sunbeam's over-sized 1996 restructuring reserve, reversed in 1997 to manufacture a turnaround.

---

## Cross-cutting tells for the whole EM family
- **Earnings rising while operating cash flow lags** is the master signal — almost every EM technique creates this gap.
- **Working-capital line items (receivables, inventory, deferred items, reserves) drifting away from revenue** is the second-order signal.
- **Accounting-policy and estimate changes that all happen to help** — when every judgment call breaks in the favorable direction, the pattern itself is the flag.
