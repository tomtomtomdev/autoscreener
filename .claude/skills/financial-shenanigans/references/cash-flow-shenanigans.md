# Cash Flow (CF) Shenanigans

Investors trust **cash flow from operations (CFO)** precisely because it's supposedly harder to fake than earnings. That trust is the target. CF shenanigans don't usually create cash out of nothing — they *relocate* it, moving inflows into the operating section or outflows out of it, so CFO looks stronger and more sustainable than the underlying business warrants.

The unifying detection idea: the **statement of cash flows has three sections — Operating, Investing, Financing — and the total is the same no matter how things are classified.** Shenanigans live in the *classification*. So always reconcile CFO against net income, and watch for items that have wandered between sections.

Schilit numbers these CF1–CF4.

## CF1 — Shifting financing cash inflows to the operating section

**What it is:** Dressing up borrowed money (a financing inflow) as if it were generated by operations.

**How it's done:**
- Recording **bank borrowings or a loan** as an operating inflow.
- **Selling/securitizing/factoring receivables** and parking the proceeds in CFO — pulling forward cash that operations would otherwise collect later, and doing it repeatedly to keep CFO up.
- **Vendor financing round-trips** — lending a customer money so they can buy your product, then booking the eventual collection as operating cash.
- Routing financing through related entities so the inflow lands in operations.

**Detection cues:**
- A spike in CFO that coincides with a jump in debt or a new securitization/factoring program (read the footnotes for "sale of receivables," "securitization," "factoring").
- DSO *falling* sharply for a non-operational reason (receivables sold, not collected).
- CFO growth that doesn't recur once the financing facility is tapped out.
- Receivables on the balance sheet dropping while sales rise.

## CF2 — Shifting normal operating cash outflows to the investing section

**What it is:** Moving ordinary operating cash *outflows* out of the operating section so CFO looks higher.

**How it's done:**
- **Capitalizing normal operating costs** so the cash outflow shows up as capex in *investing*, not as an operating outflow (the cash-flow mirror of EM4 — WorldCom did both at once).
- **"Boomerang" purchases of investments** that are really operating in nature.
- Classifying inventory or supplier purchases as investing activity.
- Using soft-dollar or capitalized-cost classifications to push routine spending into investing.

**Detection cues:**
- **Capex growing faster than revenue**, especially "capitalized software" or similar soft assets (cross-check EM4).
- A wide, widening gap between **CFO and free cash flow (CFO − capex)** — CFO looks great, FCF doesn't.
- Capitalization-policy language in the footnotes.
- Operating margin and CFO both strong while FCF and net income lag.

## CF3 — Inflating operating cash flow using acquisitions or disposals

**What it is:** Using the mechanics of M&A and divestitures to inject one-time or misclassified cash into CFO.

**How it's done:**
- **Inheriting CFO from an acquisition** — buying a company near period-end and absorbing its operating cash inflows while the cash *paid* sits in investing.
- **Acquiring contracts/receivables** so their later collection counts as organic CFO.
- Structuring an acquisition so that what's really the purchase of inventory/working capital lands as investing outflow, while the resulting sales generate operating inflows.
- Cherry-picking the timing of a disposal so operating inflows are retained but the related outflows leave with the divested unit.
- Boosting CFO by *not* paying for acquired working capital out of operations.

**Detection cues:**
- CFO jumps in the same period as a material acquisition; normalize by stripping the acquired entity's contribution.
- Serial acquirers whose **organic CFO** is hard to isolate — a recurring pattern that hides operating weakness in deal noise.
- Disclosures about acquired receivables/contracts or working-capital adjustments.
- CFO falling back once the acquisition cadence slows.

## CF4 — Boosting operating cash flow using unsustainable activities

**What it is:** Real operating cash, but pulled forward or borrowed from suppliers/customers in ways that can't continue — so the CFO trend is a mirage.

**How it's done:**
- **Stretching payables** — paying suppliers more slowly to hold cash in the period (boosts CFO now, reverses later). Watch DPO.
- **Squeezing customers / pulling collections forward** — offering discounts for early payment, or aggressive year-end collection pushes.
- **Liquidating/under-buying inventory** to free up cash (DSI falls, but it starves future sales).
- **Securitizing or selling receivables** as a recurring crutch (overlaps CF1).
- Timing tactics around the period-end close to shift cash into the right quarter.

**Detection cues:**
- **DPO rising** (paying suppliers slower), **DSI falling** unsustainably, **DSO falling** via discounts/factoring — i.e., CFO improving by working-capital squeeze rather than earnings.
- CFO strength that *can't repeat* — once payables are maxed and inventory is bled, the lever is gone.
- A one-quarter CFO surge near period-end that gives back the next period.
- Footnote mentions of changed payment terms or supplier-financing / reverse-factoring programs.

## Cross-cutting tells for the whole CF family
- **CFO consistently above net income with no clear, durable reason** deserves as much suspicion as CFO below it. "Too good" cash flow can be borrowed.
- **CFO − FCF gap**: if CFO looks healthy but free cash flow doesn't, suspect CF2 (capitalized outflows) or capex-dependent operations.
- **Reconcile CFO to NI line by line**: outsized "swings" in receivables, payables, inventory, or "other" are where relocations hide.
- **Normalize for M&A and one-offs**: always ask what *organic, recurring* operating cash the business throws off — that's the number Schilit cares about.
- **Read the financing and investing sections, not just operating** — a shenanigan is a thing that left one section and appeared in another.
