# Key Metric (KM) Shenanigans

The income statement and cash flow statement are governed by GAAP. The *metrics management chooses to headline* are not. KM shenanigans game the narrative layer — the non-GAAP measures, operational KPIs, and balance-sheet ratios that frame how investors interpret the audited numbers. Because these often sit outside GAAP, they're a favorite hiding place once the auditable statements get harder to manipulate.

Schilit splits these into two: misleading metrics that *overstate performance*, and balance-sheet metrics distorted to *avoid showing deterioration*.

## KM1 — Showcasing misleading metrics that overstate performance

**What it is:** Promoting a non-GAAP or operational metric that paints a rosier picture than GAAP — or quietly redefining a metric so growth looks better than it is.

**How it's done:**
- **"Adjusted EBITDA" / "adjusted earnings" that strip out recurring costs** — excluding stock-based comp, "one-time" charges that recur every year, restructuring that never ends, or normal operating expenses relabeled as non-core.
- **Cherry-picking the metric** — touting orders, bookings, billings, "annual recurring revenue," gross merchandise value, or active users when GAAP revenue and profit are weak.
- **Changing a metric's definition** without flagging it — redefining "active user," "same-store sales," "bookings," or the adjustment list so the new basis isn't comparable to prior periods.
- **Highlighting a metric that conveniently excludes the bad part** — e.g., revenue ex-a-declining-segment, or "constant currency" only when it helps.
- Inventing a bespoke metric with no standard definition so there's nothing to compare against.

**Detection cues:**
- The **gap between GAAP net income and the headline "adjusted" number widening** over time — and the add-backs being recurring in nature.
- Stock-based compensation as a perennial "adjustment" while it's a real, ongoing cost.
- A **metric definition that changed** (read the metric footnotes / non-GAAP reconciliation and the "how we define X" disclosure).
- Management leading earnings releases and calls with the operational metric and burying GAAP.
- Operational metrics (users, bookings, GMV) growing while GAAP revenue/profit don't follow — a value-conversion problem.
- The non-GAAP reconciliation table getting longer each year.

**Why it matters:** A pro-forma metric isn't inherently dishonest — it can clarify the business. The shenanigan is in *which* costs get excluded, *whether the definition is stable*, and *whether GAAP is being obscured*. Always rebuild the GAAP picture and compare.

## KM2 — Distorting balance-sheet metrics to avoid showing deterioration

**What it is:** Managing balance-sheet and working-capital metrics so that a deteriorating financial position doesn't show up in the ratios investors and lenders watch.

**How it's done:**
- **Selling/factoring receivables before period-end** to make DSO look better than collections justify.
- **Channel-stuffing in reverse for the metric** — managing the period-end receivable or inventory balance with timing tricks.
- **Stretching payables** to flatter working capital or liquidity ratios (mirror of CF4).
- **Reverse factoring / supplier-financing programs** that move what is economically debt off the "debt" line and into payables, understating leverage.
- Reclassifying short-term debt, or using off-balance-sheet structures, to keep leverage ratios within covenant limits.
- Timing inventory purchases/write-offs to manage turnover ratios.

**Detection cues:**
- **DSO improving via receivable sales** rather than faster collection (cross-check the cash flow statement and footnotes).
- **Payables / DPO rising** with supplier-financing or reverse-factoring disclosures — a sign reported "debt" understates true obligations.
- Working-capital or liquidity ratios that improve right at quarter-end and revert just after.
- A widening gap between reported leverage and **leverage including off-balance-sheet and supplier-financing obligations**.
- Ratios that hug covenant thresholds suspiciously closely period after period.

**Why it matters:** This family is about *concealing weakening*, not overstating strength — it's how a company nearing distress can keep looking solvent on the metrics until it suddenly isn't. Lenders and covenant analysts care most here.

## Cross-cutting tells for the whole KM family
- **Always rebuild GAAP.** For any headline non-GAAP number, find the reconciliation and ask whether the add-backs are genuinely non-recurring and economically non-cash. Recurring "one-timers" are the tell.
- **Check metric stability over time.** A definition change is the single most common KM trick — quietly recut "active users" or "bookings" and growth reappears.
- **Watch where economic debt hides.** Reverse factoring and operating-lease/off-balance-sheet structures move real obligations off the leverage ratios. Reconstruct true leverage.
- **KM rarely travels alone.** A flattering metric usually accompanies EM or CF strain — the metric is the story that explains away the GAAP weakness. Triangulate across all three families.
