# CFA Preparation

A mentor's roadmap for earning the CFA charter — the most rigorous, globally recognized credential for the *investor/analyst* path (as opposed to the client-advising path, which is CFP/local licensing). Administered by the **CFA Institute**.

## Verify before relying on specifics

Exam windows, fees, topic weights, and requirements change regularly, and the CFA Institute has been actively reworking the program (Practical Skills Modules, Level III specialization pathways). Treat the structure below as a study map, not current logistics — **confirm every date, fee, and requirement at cfainstitute.org** before the learner commits.

## What it is and who it's for

A three-level, self-study program in investment analysis, valuation, ethics, and portfolio management. Best for people who want to *analyze securities and manage money* to a professional standard. It is demanding (commonly cited at ~300 hours per level) and takes most candidates two to four years across all three levels.

## The path to the charter (four parts)

1. **Pass all three exam levels** (sequentially — must pass each to sit the next).
2. **Qualified work experience:** on the order of 4,000 hours of investment-decision-related work over a minimum of ~36 months (verify current rule).
3. **References and membership:** professional references plus CFA Institute membership.
4. **Ethics:** commit to the Code of Ethics and Standards of Professional Conduct.

Eligibility to *enroll* generally requires a bachelor's degree, being within ~11 months of graduating, or a qualifying mix of work/education — confirm current entrance criteria.

## The three levels

- **Level I — Foundations.** Knowledge and comprehension. Multiple-choice, computer-based, offered in several windows per year. Broadest coverage of the ten topic areas.
- **Level II — Asset valuation.** Application and analysis, delivered as "item sets" (a vignette followed by linked questions). This is the valuation-heavy level.
- **Level III — Portfolio management & wealth planning.** Synthesis, including constructed-response (essay) questions alongside item sets. The CFA Institute has introduced **specialization pathways** at this level (e.g. portfolio management, private markets, private wealth) — verify current options.

In addition, **Practical Skills Modules (PSMs)** are required components candidates complete to be eligible to sit, reflecting the program's shift toward applied skills. Confirm which are currently required.

## Level I topic areas (approximate weights — verify)

Ethical & Professional Standards (~15–20%, weighted heaviest for a reason), Quantitative Methods, Economics, Financial Statement Analysis, Corporate Issuers, Equity Investments, Fixed Income, Derivatives, Alternative Investments, and Portfolio Management. Weights shift level to level — Ethics stays large throughout, valuation grows at Level II, portfolio construction dominates Level III.

## How to prepare (sequencing)

1. **Start with Ethics and keep returning to it** — it's heavily weighted and decides borderline passes.
2. **Build the quantitative/accounting base early** (Quant, Economics, Financial Statement Analysis) since later topics lean on it.
3. **Do problems, not just reading.** The exams test application; passive reading produces recognition, not the ability to solve item sets under time pressure.
4. **Mock exams late.** In the final 4–6 weeks, sit full-length timed mocks and review every miss to a root cause.
5. **Respect the hours.** Spread ~300 hours over ~4–6 months per level; cramming rarely works at this depth.

## Reputable materials

- **CFA Institute official curriculum and Learning Ecosystem** (included with registration) — the authoritative source; the exam is written from it.
- **Reputable third-party prep** (supplements, not replacements): Kaplan Schweser, Mark Meldrum, Wiley, AnalystPrep, Bloomberg Exam Prep. Choose one, don't collect several.
- Be skeptical of "guaranteed pass" shortcuts and brain-dump question banks of unknown provenance.

## How this connects to the broader curriculum

CFA Level I–II material overlaps heavily with Phases 0–5 of the `investing-curriculum` skill (accounting literacy, valuation mechanics, equity/fixed income, portfolio theory). A sensible order for a self-learner: work the curriculum's analytical phases first, then sit Level I, by which point much of the quantitative and accounting content is review. The CFA adds breadth (fixed income, derivatives, alternatives, ethics) and a recognized credential; the curriculum's value-investing depth and forensic-accounting phase complement it.
