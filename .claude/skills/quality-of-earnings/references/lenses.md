# The Eight Lenses — Detailed Method

Full procedure for each lens. For each: what it detects, how to run it, the thresholds, the benign explanations to rule out, and the footnote/disclosure to cite.

## 1. Nonrecurring & nonoperating income

**Detects:** earnings growth that comes from items that won't repeat, dressed up as if it were operating performance.

**Run it:**
- Comb the income statement and footnotes for: gains on sale of assets/divisions/securities, litigation or insurance settlements, gains on early debt retirement, one-time tax benefits, pension income flowing into operating results, gains from changes in accounting principle, reversals of prior reserves, and anything labeled "other income," "special," or "nonrecurring."
- For each, estimate its after-tax contribution to EPS.
- Subtract them from reported earnings to get **core operating earnings**.
- Compare core-earnings growth to reported growth. If reported growth depends on these items, the headline overstates true momentum.

**Watch especially:**
- A company that books "nonrecurring" gains *every year* — recurring nonrecurring items are an operating-quality red flag and a management-credibility tell.
- Gains buried in "other income" rather than disclosed as special items.
- Operating expenses pushed into a "restructuring charge" while the offsetting benefit quietly flows through future operating lines.

**Benign explanations:** a genuinely one-off event (a single divestiture) is fine *as long as it's identified and excluded* — the issue is only when it's blended into the trend.

## 2. Accounts receivable vs. sales

**Detects:** revenue that isn't converting to cash — channel-stuffing, premature/aggressive revenue recognition, looser credit to push sales, or customers in distress. Often a leading indicator of a future sales shortfall or a bad-debt write-off.

**Run it:**
- Compute AR growth % and sales growth % over the same periods. Compute days sales outstanding (DSO = AR / Sales × days) and trend it.
- **Threshold:** AR growing meaningfully faster than sales for one period = 🟡; a *sustained* gap (2–3+ periods) or a sharp DSO jump = 🔴.
- Check the allowance for doubtful accounts: shrinking as a % of receivables while receivables age is a quiet earnings boost.

**Benign explanations to rule out:** a mid-year acquisition (acquired receivables inflate the balance), a deliberate expansion into markets/customers with longer payment terms, strong sales concentrated late in the period, or a shift in customer mix. Distinguish by checking whether the build is broad-based and persistent vs. one-time and explained.

## 3. Inventory vs. sales

**Detects:** overproduction and demand softening before it hits the income statement; future margin cuts and write-downs; non-repeatable LIFO effects.

**Run it:**
- Compute inventory growth % vs. sales (and ideally vs. COGS) growth %. Compute inventory turnover (COGS / avg inventory) and days inventory; trend both.
- **Threshold:** inventory outgrowing sales = 🟡; sustained, or with falling turnover = 🔴.
- **Category mix (if disclosed):** raw materials / work-in-process / finished goods. A build concentrated in **finished goods** is the most ominous — it means product isn't selling, not that the company is gearing up.
- **LIFO vs. FIFO (footnote):** under inflation, FIFO flows old, cheap costs to COGS and inflates margins with "phantom" inventory profits; LIFO is more conservative. Note the method.
- **LIFO liquidation:** if a LIFO company draws down inventory, it sells old low-cost layers, which *temporarily* boosts gross profit. Footnotes disclose the effect. This is a non-repeatable earnings boost — strip it from the trend.

**Benign explanations:** deliberate pre-build for a known launch or seasonal peak, supply-chain stockpiling, or an acquisition. Look for management explanation and whether finished goods specifically are the driver.

## 4. Depreciation & amortization

**Detects:** earnings borrowed from the future by under-charging for the consumption of assets.

**Run it:**
- Read the depreciation footnote across years. Flag any of: **extending estimated useful lives**, **switching from accelerated to straight-line**, **raising assumed salvage values**, reclassifying assets to longer-lived categories, or capitalizing costs that were previously expensed. Each *reduces* current expense and lifts reported earnings without any change in economics.
- Compare D&A to capex over time. Persistent D&A far below capex can mean a growing asset base (fine) — but D&A *falling* while the asset base grows warrants a look at the assumptions.
- Quantify the EPS effect of any disclosed change and treat it as low-quality (non-economic) earnings.

**Benign explanations:** genuine changes in asset life (e.g., longer-lived technology, a real change in usage patterns) can be legitimate — but the burden is on the disclosure to justify it, and the timing (does it conveniently rescue an earnings miss?) is informative.

## 5. Discretionary ("future-directed") costs

**Detects:** short-term earnings bought by starving the future — R&D, advertising, marketing, maintenance, and similar spending that builds future revenue.

**Run it:**
- Track each available discretionary line as a **% of sales** over multiple years.
- A sudden contraction — especially one that coincides with hitting an earnings target or offsetting weakness elsewhere — is low-quality earnings: the profit is real this period but mortgages future competitiveness.
- Conversely, heavy *current* spending that depresses reported earnings can mask high underlying quality; note it as a positive quality signal even if it dents the headline.

**Benign explanations:** efficiency gains, a deliberate strategic pullback from a failing initiative, or completion of a one-time campaign. Ask whether the cut is structural and disclosed vs. an opportunistic year-end shave.

## 6. Taxes

**Detects:** EPS growth manufactured by a falling tax rate, and aggressive book accounting revealed by the book-vs-tax gap. (O'Glove considered the tax footnote one of the most revealing pages in the report.)

**Run it:**
- Compute the **effective tax rate** (tax expense / pretax income) each year and compare to the statutory rate. A *declining* effective rate flatters EPS growth that has nothing to do with operations.
- Read the rate-reconciliation footnote to separate **sustainable** drivers (geographic mix, permanent structural items) from **one-time** benefits (NOL carryforwards being used up, expiring credits, audit settlements, a one-off repatriation). Normalize EPS to a sustainable tax rate to see underlying growth.
- Track **deferred tax liabilities.** A persistently rising DTL means book income is being reported faster than taxable income — i.e., management's accounting to shareholders is more aggressive than what it tells the tax authority. A large/growing gap is a quality warning.

**Benign explanations:** a real, durable shift in jurisdiction mix or a legislated rate change is legitimate — but should still be normalized out of *growth* comparisons.

## 7. Cash flow vs. earnings

**Detects:** the master quality check. Accrual earnings can be managed; cash is harder to fake. Divergence between the two is the strongest single signal of low quality.

**Run it:**
- Compare net income to **cash flow from operations (OCF)** over multiple years. Healthy businesses convert earnings to cash; over time OCF should roughly track or exceed net income.
- **Red flag:** net income rising while OCF stalls or falls. Reconcile the gap — it usually traces back to ballooning receivables, inventory, or other accruals (tie this back to lenses 2 and 3).
- Compute the **accrual ratio** ((Net income − OCF) / average total assets, or the balance-sheet accrual version). Large positive accruals = earnings running ahead of cash = lower quality and historically associated with weaker future returns.
- Compute **free cash flow** (OCF − capex). Earnings that never become free cash are suspect.
- Watch for cash-flow-statement gamesmanship too: classifying recurring outflows as investing/financing, or stretching payables to flatter OCF.

**Benign explanations:** a fast-growing company legitimately consumes cash in working capital; a heavy-capex build-out depresses FCF for good reasons. The question is whether the *gap is explained by growth* or by deteriorating collections/unsold inventory.

## 8. Hidden liabilities & off-statement risk

**Detects:** earnings and obligations that live in the footnotes rather than the face of the statements.

**Run it — check each disclosure:**
- **Pension / OPEB assumptions.** An aggressive *expected return on plan assets* or a high *discount rate* lowers pension expense (or creates pension income) and lifts reported earnings without operating substance. Compare assumptions to peers and to reality; flag overfunded-plan income flowing into the income statement.
- **Capitalize vs. expense.** Costs capitalized (software development, interest, certain marketing/subscriber-acquisition costs) move expense off today's income statement onto the balance sheet, to be amortized later. Flag changes in capitalization policy.
- **Reserve / "cookie-jar" accounting.** Big reserves taken in good times and released into earnings in weak quarters smooth and inflate results. Watch reserve balances and releases.
- **Off-balance-sheet obligations.** Operating leases, guarantees, special-purpose entities, securitizations, purchase commitments — obligations that don't show as debt but are real.
- **Shareholder-letter read (qualitative).** Compare this year's letter to prior years. Look for: segments or metrics that *disappear* from the discussion, blame shifted to external factors, a tone that contradicts the footnotes, and emphasis on a non-GAAP metric while GAAP weakens. What management stops talking about is often what's deteriorating.

**Benign explanations:** capitalization and lease structures can be entirely standard for the industry — compare to peers before flagging, and focus on *changes* in policy and *outlier* assumptions.

---

## Putting it together

No single lens is decisive. Build the verdict from the *pattern*: a company stripping discretionary costs, stretching depreciation lives, riding a falling tax rate, with receivables and inventory outrunning sales and OCF lagging net income, is telling one coherent story — earnings are being manufactured and the future is being borrowed against. A company with conservative accounting, cash-backed earnings, and steady investment is telling the opposite. Score each lens, weight cash flow and the receivables/inventory checks most heavily, and write the synthesis.
