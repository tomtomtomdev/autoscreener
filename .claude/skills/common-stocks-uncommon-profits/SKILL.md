# Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits — Philip A. Fisher Framework

Apply the framework from *Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits* by Philip A. Fisher (1958,
revised 1996) to identify businesses with exceptional long-run growth potential and the
management capable of sustaining it. Fisher is the intellectual foundation of qualitative
growth investing — the counterpart to Graham's quantitative value approach.

> *"The stock market is filled with individuals who know the price of everything, but the
> value of nothing."* — Philip Fisher (paraphrasing Oscar Wilde for investing)

**Core premise:** The greatest investment returns come not from buying cheap assets, but
from identifying exceptional businesses early and holding them through temporary adversity.
Price matters, but quality of business and management matters far more.

---

## Fisher vs. Graham — When to Use Which

| Dimension | Fisher (This Skill) | Graham (Security Analysis Skill) |
|---|---|---|
| Primary lens | Qualitative business quality | Quantitative asset/earnings safety |
| Holding period | Decades | Until price recovers to value |
| What you're buying | Future earning power | Present undervaluation |
| Management weight | Central, decisive | Secondary |
| Growth emphasis | Essential | Optional / distrusted |
| Typical company | Excellent but not cheap | Cheap, often mediocre |
| Biggest risk of error | Overpaying for a good business | Holding a value trap |
| IDX application | Consumer staples, digital, healthcare growth | Cyclicals, commodity, conglomerates |

**Key distinction:** Graham asks "is this cheap?" Fisher asks "is this great?" The best
investors (Buffett's own words) use both. For any IDX growth analysis, read this skill.
For deep value / cyclical / bond analysis, use the security-analysis skill.

## Part I — The 15 Points: Evaluating Business Quality

Fisher's 15 points are a qualitative checklist for identifying companies worth owning for
the long term. No company scores perfectly on all 15 — but outstanding companies score
well on most, especially 1, 2, 4, 5, 8, 9, 10, and 13.

For each point, apply the **Scuttlebutt method** (see Part II) to gather evidence beyond
what management tells you.

---

### Point 1: Does the company have products or services with sufficient market potential to make possible a sizeable increase in sales for at least several years?

**What to assess:**
- Is the addressable market large, growing, or capable of being expanded?
- Can the company grow within its existing business, or must it constantly acquire new markets?
- Are we early, mid, or late in the growth runway?

**Red flags:**
- Market is mature or structurally shrinking
- Growth depends entirely on adjacent M&A (not organic)
- Company's growth is market-share theft, not market expansion — sustainable only until competition responds

**IDX context:** Indonesian consumer, healthcare, and digital sectors still have long runways
due to demographics and underpenetration. Evaluate against per-capita benchmarks vs. peer
markets (Malaysia, Thailand).

---

### Point 2: Does management have a determination to continue to develop products or processes that will still further increase total sales potentials when the growth potentials of currently attractive product lines have largely been exploited?

**What to assess:**
- R&D pipeline: Is there a next-generation product being developed?
- Does the company reinvest profits into innovation, or extract them?
- Is management thinking 5–10 years ahead, or just the next quarter?

**Key signal:** Companies that solve "what's next" before the current wave peaks. Intel in the
80s, Astra in the 90s, BCA through every fintech disruption cycle.

**Red flag:** Reliance on a single product with no visible successor.

---

### Point 3: How effective are the company's research and development efforts in relation to its size?

**What to assess:**
- R&D spend as % of revenue (compare to industry peers, not absolute number)
- Output per R&D dollar — how many successful products per IDR X spent?
- Time from concept to commercial product
- Is R&D centralized or spread across business units?

**Fisher's warning:** High R&D spend alone means nothing. Effectiveness is what matters.
A company spending 2% of revenue on R&D that generates successful products beats one
spending 8% that produces failures.

**IDX note:** Formal R&D is rare among most IDX companies. For consumer/FMCG, substitute
with product development investment, brand portfolio extension, and distribution innovation.

---

### Point 4: Does the company have an above-average sales organization?

**What to assess:**
- Is the salesforce respected by customers (relationship-driven vs. transactional)?
- After-sale service quality — does the company solve problems quickly?
- Customer retention rate and net promoter score
- Do customers buy again and again, or is it one-time?

**Signal:** In B2B, talk to customers. In B2C, look at churn rates, app reviews, net
promoter scores, and repeat purchase data.

**IDX context:** Distribution strength is a massive moat in Indonesia. Companies with
dominant last-mile distribution (Unilever, ICBP, SIDO) hold structural advantage because
distribution cannot be replicated quickly even with capital.

---

### Point 5: Does the company have a worthwhile profit margin?

**What to assess:**
- Is the operating margin above the industry average?
- Is the margin trend expanding, stable, or compressing?
- Can the company raise prices without losing volume? (pricing power)
- Does management resist the temptation to grow revenue at the expense of margins?

**Fisher's test:** A company that grows revenue 20% but shrinks margins 5 points is not
creating value — it's buying market share. Reject it.

**Formulas:**
```
Gross Margin = (Revenue − COGS) / Revenue
Operating Margin = EBIT / Revenue
Net Margin = Net Income / Revenue
```

**IDX benchmark ranges (rough):**
| Sector | Healthy Operating Margin |
|---|---|
| Consumer staples (ICBP, MYOR) | 12–20% |
| Banking (BBCA, BMRI) | 30–45% ROE proxy |
| Healthcare (KLBF, SIDO) | 15–25% |
| Tech/digital | Variable; prioritize trajectory |
| Mining | 20–35% (mid-cycle only) |

---

### Point 6: What is the company doing to maintain or improve profit margins?

**What to assess:**
- Cost reduction roadmap (automation, supply chain, manufacturing efficiency)
- Product mix shift toward higher-margin products
- Pricing strategy evolution
- Management discussion in annual reports: Are they specific or vague about margin improvement?

**Fisher's distinction:** Margin improvement through productivity and pricing power =
excellent. Margin improvement by cutting costs in sales, R&D, or quality = dangerous.

---

### Point 7: Does the company have outstanding labor and personnel relations?

**What to assess:**
- Strike history, labor dispute frequency
- Employee turnover rate vs. industry peers
- Management promotion from within vs. constant external hiring
- Employee satisfaction signals (Glassdoor equivalent, LinkedIn employee reviews)
- How did the company treat employees during the 2020–2022 COVID period?

**Fisher's logic:** Poor labor relations surface eventually as productivity decline, quality
problems, or sudden talent exodus — all of which hurt earnings unpredictably.

**IDX note:** Indonesian labor law (UU Cipta Kerja) and union dynamics differ from Western
markets. For manufacturing companies, check BPJS compliance, PHK (layoff) history, and
whether there are active SPSI union disputes.

---

### Point 8: Does the company have outstanding executive relations?

**What to assess:**
- Do senior executives stay long? (High turnover at C-level = warning)
- Are executives fairly compensated without being excessively paid?
- Is there a genuine succession plan, or is the company CEO-dependent?
- Does middle management have authority and initiative?

**Fisher's observation:** The best companies promote internal talent. Companies that must
constantly hire executives externally signal that internal talent is not developed or
retained — a cultural failure.

---

### Point 9: Does the company have depth to its management?

**What to assess:**
- If the CEO left tomorrow, would the company survive and thrive?
- Are there capable second and third-tier leaders driving results?
- Is decision-making centralized in one person, or distributed?

**Red flag for IDX family conglomerates:** Companies where the founder's children hold
all key positions regardless of competence — succession-by-bloodline rather than
succession-by-merit. (This is common on IDX; not automatically disqualifying, but requires
extra scrutiny of management track record.)

---

### Point 10: How good are the company's cost analysis and accounting controls?

**What to assess:**
- Does management know the true cost of each product line?
- Are cost overruns identified and corrected quickly?
- Is budgeting rigorous and are variance explanations substantive?
- Quality of notes to financial statements — specific or generic?

**Fisher's key insight:** A company that doesn't know where it's making and losing money
cannot manage profitably at scale. This is an operational failure that eventually becomes
a financial failure.

---

### Point 11: Are there other aspects of the business, somewhat peculiar to the industry involved, that will give the investor important clues as to how outstanding the company may be in relation to its competition?

**What to assess:**
- Sector-specific moats: patents, regulatory licenses, network effects, switching costs,
  proprietary data, brand heritage
- Economies of scale that create cost advantage
- Location advantages (IDX: geographic dominance in Java vs. Outer Islands)

**IDX examples:**
- Banking: CASA ratio (low-cost funding moat) — BBCA's ~80% CASA is exceptional
- Consumer: route-to-market density in Tier 2/3 cities
- Healthcare: doctor relationship networks, regulatory approvals pipeline
- Digital: data network effects, API integration lock-in

---

### Point 12: Does the company have a short-range or long-range outlook in regard to profits?

**What to assess:**
- Does management sacrifice long-term positioning for short-term EPS beats?
- R&D cuts before earnings pressure = red flag
- Marketing cuts before earnings pressure = red flag
- Are capex decisions oriented toward 3–5 year returns, or just this year's FCF?

**Signal:** Read 5 years of management commentary (AR letters, earnings calls). Do they
discuss long-term positioning? Or only "maximizing shareholder value" near-term?

---

### Point 13: In the foreseeable future will the growth of the company necessitate sufficient equity financing so that the larger number of shares outstanding will largely cancel the benefit to existing shareholders from this anticipated growth?

**What to assess:**
- Does the business require constant equity dilution to fund growth?
- What is the capital intensity of growth? (capex per IDR revenue growth)
- Can growth be funded from internal cash flow (self-funding)?
- Rights issue history on IDX — are rights issues dilutive or value-creating?

**Fisher's ideal:** A company that can fund its own growth from operations without going
back to shareholders. This is the mark of a truly exceptional business.

**Formula:**
```
Capital Intensity = Capex / Revenue Growth (IDR)
Self-Funding Ratio = Free Cash Flow / Capex + Working Capital Increase
```

**IDX check:** Look for companies that issue rights frequently for working capital (weak).
Contrast with companies like BBCA that fund expansion from deposit growth (strong).

---

### Point 14: Does management talk freely to investors about its affairs when things are going well but clam up or become evasive when troubles and disappointments occur?

**What to assess:**
- Compare management commentary during good years vs. bad years
- Do they take responsibility for failures or blame external factors only?
- Are profit warnings timely, or does bad news emerge suddenly?
- Public statements: specific and measurable, or vague and promotional?

**Fisher's principle:** Management that hides bad news will eventually surprise you with
catastrophe. Management that communicates honestly through difficulty is trustworthy.

**IDX practice:** Read press releases and keterbukaan informasi (OJK disclosures) during
2020 COVID period. How companies communicated through crisis reveals character.

---

### Point 15: Does the company have management of unquestionable integrity?

**What to assess:**
- Related party transaction pattern — fair pricing or self-dealing?
- Has the controlling shareholder ever diluted minority shareholders unfairly?
- Dividend policy: consistent and predictable, or erratic?
- Has management ever been investigated by OJK or Bapepam?
- Do insiders buy or sell stock? (Insider purchase = confidence; consistent selling = warning)

**Fisher's view:** This is non-negotiable. If management integrity is questionable, no
amount of business quality compensates. Walk away.

**IDX-specific:** Check OJK sanksi (sanction) database. Check KSEI for whether controlling
shareholder has pledged shares (collateral pledging = leverage + potential forced selling).

## Part II — The Scuttlebutt Method

Fisher's research methodology: **talk to people in and around the business** to gather
information that financial statements cannot provide.

### Who to talk to (or research as proxy)

| Source | What They Reveal |
|---|---|
| Competitors | Honest view of a company's strengths and weaknesses — competitors know |
| Customers | Product quality, pricing power, service reliability, switching intent |
| Former employees | Culture, management quality, internal dysfunction |
| Suppliers | Payment behavior, volume trajectory, reliability as customer |
| Industry analysts | Market structure, competitive dynamics, regulatory trends |
| Academic/trade publications | Technology trends, R&D effectiveness, patent landscape |

### Proxy research for retail investors

When direct scuttlebutt isn't accessible, use these proxies:
- **App Store reviews** (Google Play, iOS) — for consumer/digital companies
- **LinkedIn employee posts** — culture, layoffs, leadership changes
- **Jobstreet/Glints job postings** — company growth areas and pain points
- **Twitter/X industry discourse** — customer complaints, product feedback
- **Competitor annual reports** — indirect intelligence on market dynamics
- **Broker research notes** — often contain scuttlebutt from channel checks
- **IDX supplier disclosure** — public companies reporting major customer concentration

### Scuttlebutt Red Flags

- Competitors consistently underestimate or dismiss a company = **significant positive signal**
- Former employees (especially mid-level) speak negatively about management culture
- Customers mention plans to switch vendors or reduce reliance
- Suppliers report slowing orders before official earnings disappointment
- Industry insiders describe the company's product as "behind the curve"

## Part III — When to Buy

Fisher was not a market timer. His guidance on when to buy:

### Price vs. Quality

Fisher accepted paying a fair or even above-average price for an exceptional business.
He explicitly rejected trying to buy exceptional companies at bargain prices, because
they rarely become truly cheap.

> *"I don't want a lot of good investments; I want a few outstanding ones."*

**Fisher's buying conditions:**
1. The business passes most of the 15 Points
2. Management is demonstrably excellent and honest
3. The price is not wildly excessive relative to earning power
4. You have done genuine Scuttlebutt research, not just read the annual report

**When Fisher would pass even on a good company:**
- Integrity concerns on management (Point 15 override)
- No identifiable growth runway for at least 5 years (Point 1 failure)
- Business requires constant equity dilution (Point 13 failure)
- Price is so extreme that even perfect execution won't deliver adequate returns

### Using P/E Thoughtfully (not mechanically)

Fisher did not apply rigid P/E thresholds (unlike Graham). He evaluated P/E relative to:
- Growth rate (PEG ratio as rough guide — not a rule)
- Industry norms
- Quality premium justified by the business

```
PEG Ratio = P/E ÷ Expected Annual EPS Growth Rate
PEG < 1.0 = Potentially undervalued for growth
PEG 1.0–1.5 = Fairly valued if business quality is high
PEG > 2.0 = Requires exceptional confidence in growth durability
```

**Fisher's warning:** Don't let a high P/E scare you away from an excellent company if
growth is genuinely durable and large. Don't let a low P/E attract you to a mediocre
company.

## Part IV — When to Sell

Fisher's philosophy on selling is almost as important as his buying philosophy, and
more counterintuitive.

### The Three Valid Reasons to Sell

**Reason 1: You made a mistake in the original analysis**
- The business is not as strong as you thought
- A 15-Points item you evaluated positively turns out to be wrong
- Action: Sell promptly. Don't average down on a thesis that was wrong.

**Reason 2: The company has deteriorated**
- Management quality has declined (key executive departure, integrity breach)
- Competitive position has eroded (competitors gaining share, product obsolescence)
- Growth runway has closed or shrunk materially
- Action: Sell, even at a loss if necessary.

**Reason 3: A clearly superior opportunity exists**
- You find a company that passes the 15 Points at a significantly better price/quality ratio
- But only if you're highly confident — don't churn the portfolio on marginal differences

### What Are NOT Valid Reasons to Sell

- **Price has risen significantly** — If the business quality is intact, price appreciation
  alone is not a reason to sell. This is Fisher's most important and least-followed rule.

- **Market looks overvalued** — Fisher explicitly rejected market timing. Outstanding
  businesses compound regardless of starting conditions over a 10+ year horizon.

- **Temporary earnings disappointment** — If fundamentals are intact and the shortfall
  is genuinely temporary, a pullback is a buying opportunity, not a sell signal.

- **You need to show gains** — Selling winners to lock in profits is emotionally satisfying
  but mathematically destructive. You pay taxes and lose your compounding vehicle.

### The Sell Decision Framework

```
Question 1: Has management integrity changed? → YES = SELL IMMEDIATELY
Question 2: Has the competitive moat eroded structurally? → YES = SELL
Question 3: Has the growth runway closed (TAM saturated, disrupted)? → YES = SELL
Question 4: Was my original thesis wrong? → YES = SELL, QUICKLY
Question 5: Is it just a temporary bad quarter with fundamentals intact? → NO = HOLD
Question 6: Has the stock price risen a lot? → Alone = NOT a reason to sell
```

## Part V — Portfolio Construction (Fisher's Approach)

Fisher advocated **concentration**, not diversification. His view:

> *"Diversification is a protection against ignorance. It makes little sense if you know
> what you are doing."*

### Fisher's portfolio principles:

- **10–12 companies maximum** for a serious investor who does real Scuttlebutt research
- **3–4 core positions** that you know exceptionally well — these should be your largest holdings
- **Avoid over-diversification** — owning 50 stocks is an admission that you haven't
  done deep research on any of them
- **Size positions by conviction** — the best ideas get the most capital
- **Never over-concentrate** — even the best idea can be wrong; no single position
  should be catastrophic if it's a mistake

### IDX Portfolio Application

For a Fisher-style IDX growth portfolio:
- Focus on 8–12 companies in consumer, healthcare, digital, and financial sectors
- Avoid commodity-dependent and cyclical companies (these are Graham territory)
- Weight toward companies with 10+ year growth runway and exceptional management
- Be willing to hold through IDX volatility without panic selling

### Quick Sell Checklist

Run this before deciding to sell any Fisher-style holding:

```
Company: [Ticker]
Trigger for review: [Price drop / News event / Earnings miss / Other]

[ ] Integrity breach? (YES = immediate sell)
[ ] Management quality permanently declined?
[ ] Growth runway structurally closed?
[ ] Original thesis was factually wrong?
[ ] Competitive moat eroded — evidence, not fear?

If none of the above: What is the actual reason I'm considering selling?
[ ] Price declined (not a reason)
[ ] Market fear (not a reason)
[ ] Temporary earnings miss with intact fundamentals (not a reason)
[ ] Someone on social media / TV said to sell (not a reason)

DECISION: HOLD / SELL + Reason
```

## When to Combine with Security Analysis Skill

Fisher and Graham are complements, not substitutes. Use both when:

- You want to buy an excellent business but need to verify the balance sheet isn't hiding danger (Graham's toolkit on balance sheet quality + Fisher's 15 Points)
- A company passes 15 Points but the valuation seems stretched (Graham's EPV as sanity check)
- You're analyzing an IDX company with strong growth profile but a family controlling structure with RPT risk (Fisher for business quality, Graham for governance red flags)

> Buffett: *"I'm 85% Fisher and 15% Graham."*
