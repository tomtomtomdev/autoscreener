# Concept Glossary

Each entry names the idea, the year it was most clearly articulated in the Berkshire
letters (approximate; many recur across years), and what it means in practice. Read this
when you want deeper context on a single concept than the SKILL.md body provides.

## Mental models & philosophy

**Mr. Market (1987).** Borrowed from Graham. Imagine your business partner is a
manic-depressive who shows up daily quoting a price to buy your share or sell you his. Some
days euphoric, some despairing. His quotes are an *option* for you to transact, never a
verdict on what your business is worth. Exploit his moods; never be governed by them.

**Margin of safety (recurring; Graham's phrase).** The three most important words in
investing. The cushion between the price you pay and your estimate of intrinsic value. It
is the defense against the inevitability of being wrong about the future.

**Circle of competence (1996).** You do not need to understand every business. You need to
know the *perimeter* of what you understand and stay inside it. "What counts for most
people in investing is not how much they know, but rather how realistically they define
what they don't know." Size of the circle is irrelevant; knowing its boundary is everything.

**Wonderful business at a fair price (1989).** Crediting Munger's influence: "It's far
better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful
price." The early-career cigar-butt approach (buy terrible businesses cheap for one last
puff) gives way to paying up for quality that compounds.

**Time and business quality (1989).** "Time is the friend of the wonderful business, the
enemy of the mediocre." A great franchise compounds owners' capital; a poor business
erodes it while management "solves" problems that recur.

**Volatility is not risk (1993).** Beta and price wiggle are not risk. Risk is the
probability of permanent loss of capital or an inadequate real return. Assess it through
business economics, management, price, and inflation/taxes — not a statistics package.

**Aesop / the bird-in-the-bush (2000).** The formula for valuing any financial asset is
unchanged since ~600 B.C.: "a bird in the hand is worth two in the bush." To act, answer:
How certain are you the birds are in the bush? When and how many will emerge? What is the
risk-free rate? Quantify those and every opportunity becomes comparable on one yardstick.

## Business quality

**Economic franchise vs. business (1991).** A franchise comes from a product/service that
is (1) needed or desired, (2) believed to have no close substitute, and (3) not price-
regulated. Franchises earn high returns, can raise prices, and tolerate poor management. A
plain business earns excess returns only as the low-cost operator or amid tight supply, and
is unforgiving of weak management.

**Moat (recurring).** The structural feature that protects franchise economics from
competitors. Sources Buffett cites: low-cost scale, brand and "share of mind," switching
costs, network/regulatory position. Test by imagining a well-funded rival's assault.

**The Inevitables (1996).** The handful of businesses whose dominance over an investing
lifetime is near-certain — Coca-Cola and Gillette in his framing. Most companies are not
Inevitables; he warns against over-applying the label and notes even Inevitables can be
overpaid for.

**Economic goodwill (1983, See's Candies appendix).** The capacity to earn far-above-market
returns on modest tangible assets. Unlike amortizing accounting goodwill, genuine economic
goodwill tends to grow and is especially valuable in inflation, when a capital-light
franchise's earnings rise without proportionate reinvestment.

**Owner earnings (1986, Scott Fetzer appendix).** Reported earnings + non-cash charges −
maintenance capex − incremental working capital needed to hold unit volume. The true cash
an owner can extract. The hard, important input is maintenance (not growth) capex.

**Look-through earnings (1980s).** Reported earnings plus the company's share of investees'
*retained* earnings, less the tax that would apply if distributed. Captures economic
earning power that the dividend line hides.

## Capital allocation & management

**Capital allocation as the CEO's first job (1987).** Over a decade a company retaining 10%
of equity each year deploys ~60% of the capital at work — yet many CEOs reach the top via
operations, marketing, or politics and are poor allocators. Judge management primarily here.

**The one-dollar test (1983 onward).** Retain earnings only if each retained dollar creates
at least a dollar of market value, measured over rolling multi-year periods. Otherwise pay
out or repurchase shares below intrinsic value.

**Dividend & buyback logic (2012).** Repurchases create value for continuing owners only
when made below intrinsic value; dividends make sense when retained capital can't clear the
one-dollar test. Both flow from the same value-per-retained-dollar discipline.

**Candor (recurring; the letters themselves are the model).** Report to owners as you would
want to be reported to: discuss businesses' real economics, own mistakes plainly, avoid
GAAP gamesmanship and "big bath" charges.

**The institutional imperative (1989, "Mistakes of the First Twenty-Five Years").** Four
forces Buffett says he underestimated: (1) organizations resist any change in current
direction; (2) projects and acquisitions materialize to absorb available funds; (3) any
business craving of the leader is quickly backed by detailed rate-of-return and strategic
studies from the troops; (4) the behavior of peer companies — expansion, acquisitions,
pay — is mindlessly imitated. Watch for all four in management and in your own reasoning.

## Insurance (Berkshire's engine)

**Float (recurring).** Money held but not owned — premiums in hand before claims are paid —
available to invest. Value depends on size and cost.

**Cost of float (recurring).** Underwriting loss ÷ average float. Negative when underwriting
is profitable, i.e. Berkshire is paid to hold investable money. The aim is large, durable,
negative- or low-cost float.

**Underwriting discipline / the Noah principle (2001).** Be willing to write far less
business when prices are inadequate; "predicting rain doesn't count, building arks does."
Discipline in pricing is what keeps the cost of float low.
