# Bias & Discipline

The randomness errors are not just statistical — they are wired in. We are built to find patterns, construct causes, and judge by outcomes. This file catalogs the specific biases *Fooled by Randomness* targets, gives Wittgenstein's ruler as a master tool, and ends with Taleb's prescription: you cannot control outcomes, only your exposure and your conduct.

## 1. The bias catalog

**Survivorship bias.** We see winners, not the graveyard. (Full treatment in `luck-vs-skill.md`.) Any inference from "successful examples" is conditioned on survival and overstates skill and predictability.

**Hindsight bias ("it was obvious").** After an event, we reconstruct the past as if it were predictable and inevitable. The clean explanation available afterward was *not* available beforehand. Test: was this called, in writing, with stakes, *before* the fact? If not, the "obviousness" is manufactured.

**Self-attribution bias.** Gains are credited to skill; losses are blamed on bad luck or "unprecedented, once-in-a-century" events. A symmetric reasoner would assign luck and skill consistently to wins and losses alike.

**Narrative fallacy.** We compress noise into a causal story because stories are memorable and randomness is not. A tidy reason for why something happened is often a retrofitted narrative, not a mechanism. Beware explanations that arrived *after* the outcome.

**The rare-event / induction error.** "It hasn't happened, so it won't." Confidence highest right before the break. (See `asymmetry-and-tails.md`.)

**Confirmation seeking.** Counting confirmations of a rule while ignoring that confirmations can't prove it and one refutation can break it. Look for the disconfirming case, not more comforting examples.

**Recency / vividness (the emotional brain).** We overweight recent, vivid, emotionally loaded events and underweight base rates. Our limbic reactions to randomness are not probabilistic; the rational override is effortful and partial.

## 2. Wittgenstein's ruler

> "Unless you have confidence in the ruler's reliability, if you use a ruler to measure a table you may also be using the table to measure the ruler."

A measurement is only as trustworthy as the instrument. When the instrument is unreliable and it reports an extreme reading, the reading often tells you more about the **instrument** than about the thing measured. Applications:

- An unreliable forecaster who turns out "right" once is weak evidence about the world and strong evidence the prediction was luck — read the result back onto the forecaster, not the future.
- A model that, calibrated on quiet data, declares a crash "impossible" and then a crash occurs: the event refutes the *model*, it doesn't make the event "a 25-sigma fluke." When the data and the model disagree at the extreme, suspect the model.
- A statistic so extreme it seems to prove a bold claim should first make you doubt the measurement process.

Master heuristic: **when reading a measurement, always ask what it implies about the measuring device.**

## 3. The expert / forecaster problem

Many domains (markets, geopolitics, macro forecasting) are dominated by randomness, so "expertise" there is largely unfalsifiable and confidence is uncorrelated with accuracy. Forecasters are rewarded for confident narratives, not calibration, and survivorship promotes whoever was recently lucky. Treat point forecasts in high-randomness domains as entertainment; weight track records by *calibration over a large sample*, not by confidence, eloquence, or a famous recent call.

## 4. Judging the decision vs. the outcome

A decision and its outcome are different objects. A sound decision (good process, favorable distribution) can yield a bad result; a reckless one can yield a great result. Judging only by outcome — **resulting** — rewards lucky fools and punishes sound operators, and teaches everyone the wrong lessons. Evaluate the *process and the exposure taken*: given what was knowable at the time and the distribution of outcomes the decision exposed you to, was it wise? The single realized branch is one draw, not the verdict.

## 5. The Stoic prescription: control exposure, not results

Taleb's constructive conclusion. You cannot control whether randomness favors you in any given history. You *can* control:

- **Your exposure.** Cap the downside so no single outcome can ruin you (avoid the absorbing barrier; prefer positive skew; barbell — see `asymmetry-and-tails.md`). Shape *which distribution* you face, since you can't choose the draw.
- **Your process.** Make decisions that are sound across the distribution of possible histories, and judge yourself on that, not on the realized branch.
- **Your conduct.** Meet good and bad luck with the same dignity. Don't let a lucky streak inflate self-attribution, or a bad draw provoke abandonment of a sound process.

The aim is not to predict the rare event but to be *robust* (ideally antifragile) to it — arranged so that being wrong is survivable and being right is rewarded. Randomness is accepted, not denied; exposure is engineered, not the future.

## 6. Diagnostic questions

- Is success being explained by a story that only became available *after* the outcome? (Narrative / hindsight.)
- Are wins and losses being attributed to luck and skill consistently, or self-servingly?
- Is a prediction being used to judge the world, when it should be used to judge the predictor? (Wittgenstein's ruler.)
- Is a decision being judged by its single outcome rather than by its process and the exposure it took on?
- Does the plan try to *predict* the rare event, or to be *robust* to it regardless of prediction?
- What is actually controllable here — the outcome (rarely) or the exposure and conduct (always)?
