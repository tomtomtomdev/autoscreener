# The Essays of Warren Buffett — Framework

Buffett's investment philosophy, distilled from his shareholder letters. The lens is: treat every stock purchase as buying a fraction of a real business, and analyze it the way a sensible private owner would.

## Step 1: Is this a business you can understand?

The circle of competence. Before analyzing, address:
- Can you describe how this company will make money 10 years from now?
- Do you understand the unit economics, competitive structure, and key risks?

If "no," the right move is pass. The circle's edge matters more than its size.

## Step 2: Does it have a durable economic moat?

The single most important business quality. Identify which moat (if any) applies and assess its durability:

- **Intangible assets** — brands, patents, regulatory licenses
- **Switching costs** — cost (financial or operational) of changing providers
- **Network effects** — value increases with users
- **Cost advantages** — scale, location, process, unique input access
- **Efficient scale** — small market that supports only a few players

For each candidate moat, ask:
- Is it widening, holding, or narrowing?
- What could destroy it? (Technology, regulation, consumer behavior shift, new entrant with different model.)
- How long has it survived already?

Buffett: "What we want is a business with a moat around it, with a very valuable castle in the middle, and someone honest and decent in charge."

## Step 3: Owner earnings

The metric that matters more than reported earnings:

**Owner earnings = Reported earnings + D&A + other non-cash charges − maintenance capex − necessary working capital investment**

Compute and compare to:
- Market cap (owner earnings yield)
- Long-term bond yields
- Historical own range

This is the cash a long-term owner could actually pull out of the business or have reinvested at company returns.

## Step 4: Return on equity and incremental capital

Buffett wants high returns on equity achieved without excessive leverage, and crucially, the ability to reinvest retained earnings at similar returns.

- **ROE** — sustained 15%+ over a full cycle, without much leverage.
- **ROIC** — return on invested capital (debt + equity); cleaner measure when capital structures vary.
- **Reinvestment runway** — can the company deploy more capital at similar returns, or is it stuck returning cash because there's nowhere to put it?

A 25% ROE business that can only reinvest 20% of earnings is less valuable than a 20% ROE business that can reinvest 80% of earnings.

## Step 5: Management quality and integrity

Buffett's three qualities for managers: intelligence, energy, and integrity. The last one is non-negotiable.

Assess:
- **Capital allocation** — what has management done with cash? Bought back stock when cheap or when high? Paid for acquisitions in stock or cash, and at what prices? Reinvested at good or poor returns?
- **Communication with shareholders** — candid annual letters, willingness to discuss mistakes openly, plain English not corporate-speak.
- **Compensation** — tied to long-term per-share value creation, not short-term metrics that can be gamed.
- **Track record** — through one or more cycles, not just the recent boom.

Read the proxy statement and the last 5–10 years of annual letters.

## Step 6: Intrinsic value

Buffett: "Intrinsic value is the discounted value of the cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life."

Estimate:
- Project owner earnings or free cash flow over the next 10 years using conservative assumptions
- Apply a terminal value tied to long-term growth and required return
- Discount back at a conservative rate (Buffett historically used the long bond plus an equity-like buffer; many practitioners use 10%)

Output a range, not a point.

## Step 7: Price discipline

Buffett: "Price is what you pay; value is what you get." Apply:
- Margin of safety vs. intrinsic value range
- Where the multiple sits relative to history and quality
- What the implied long-term return is at current price

Buffett is willing to pay full price for the right business but is rarely willing to pay any price.

## Step 8: The four filters (Munger/Buffett)

A quick final pass:
1. Can I understand it?
2. Does it have durable competitive advantages?
3. Is management able and trustworthy?
4. Is the price right?

A "no" on any filter is a pass.

## Output format

1. **Understanding** — describe the business in plain language, 10-year view.
2. **Moat** — type, durability, threats.
3. **Owner earnings** — number and yield.
4. **Returns on capital and reinvestment runway**.
5. **Management** — capital allocation record, communication, compensation, integrity.
6. **Intrinsic value range** — conservative DCF.
7. **Price vs. value** — margin of safety.
8. **Four filters** — pass/fail on each.
9. **Verdict** — wonderful business at a fair price / wonderful business but too expensive / decent business / pass.

## What to avoid

- Don't analyze inside an industry you don't understand. Pass is fine.
- Don't accept reported earnings as owner earnings. The adjustments matter.
- Don't ignore management's capital allocation history. It's the single best predictor of future per-share value creation.
- Don't pay any price for a wonderful business. Wonderful businesses at silly prices have produced poor returns historically.
- Don't confuse a long-tenured CEO with a great capital allocator. Look at outcomes, not titles.
