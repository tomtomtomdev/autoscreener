# The Psychology of Human Misjudgment — Bias Screen

Munger's catalog of the standard causes of human misjudgment. Use it as a checklist for **Track 2** analysis: run a situation (or your own reasoning) past each tendency and ask whether it is firing. You will rarely find just one — and where several reinforce each other, you have a **Lollapalooza** (Tendency 25), which is where the largest errors and the biggest manias live.

How to use: don't recite all 25 at the user. Identify the 3–6 that are actually operating in the situation, name them, explain how each distorts the specific decision, and check whether they compound.

## The 25 tendencies

1. **Reward and Punishment Superresponse Tendency.** People (and institutions) respond to incentives far more powerfully than to reason or ethics. *Investing:* read management compensation before reading the strategy deck; commission-driven advice is suspect; "the cash register is the friend of civilization" cuts both ways. *Check:* who gets paid how, and does the behavior follow the money?

2. **Liking/Loving Tendency.** We distort facts to favor people and things we like, and ignore their faults. *Investing:* falling in love with a stock, a founder, or your own past thesis. *Check:* am I being generous with the facts because I like the story or the person?

3. **Disliking/Hating Tendency.** The mirror image — we distort facts against people/things we dislike and undervalue them. *Investing:* dismissing a great business because you hate the CEO or the sector. *Check:* am I ignoring merit out of distaste?

4. **Doubt-Avoidance Tendency.** The mind rushes to remove doubt by reaching a decision. *Investing:* premature conviction; closing analysis to escape discomfort. *Check:* did I decide fast just to stop feeling uncertain? Genuine analysis sits with doubt.

5. **Inconsistency-Avoidance Tendency.** We resist changing conclusions, habits, and commitments (status-quo bias, commitment-and-consistency, confirmation bias). The first conclusion sticks hard. *Investing:* refusing to sell a loser because selling admits the original buy was wrong; anchoring on your entry price. *Check:* would I buy this today at this price knowing only what I know now? If not, holding is inconsistency-avoidance.

6. **Curiosity Tendency.** The drive to understand — one of the few *helpful* tendencies; it fuels the multidisciplinary learning the whole method depends on. *Check:* am I actually curious about disconfirming evidence, or only the confirming kind?

7. **Kantian Fairness Tendency.** Expecting and demanding fair dealing. *Investing:* outrage at "unfair" prices or terms can block rational action; markets owe you nothing.

8. **Envy/Jealousy Tendency.** Driven by others' gains. "It is not greed that drives the world, but envy." *Investing:* chasing what neighbors or rivals own; benchmark envy forcing closet-indexing or FOMO buys at tops. *Check:* do I want this because it's good, or because someone else is making money on it?

9. **Reciprocation Tendency.** We feel compelled to return favors (and slights). *Investing:* free research, lavish hospitality, and "access" buy biased loyalty; honored at how a concession triggers a counter-concession.

10. **Influence-from-Mere-Association Tendency.** We misjudge things by what they're associated with (Persian Messenger Syndrome — punishing bearers of bad news; pricing as a quality cue). *Investing:* buying a bad company because it shares a buzzword/sector with winners; conflating a great past with a great future.

11. **Simple, Pain-Avoiding Psychological Denial.** Distorting reality until it's bearable. *Investing:* refusing to see a thesis is broken; "it'll come back." *Check:* what fact am I refusing to fully look at?

12. **Excessive Self-Regard Tendency.** Overconfidence; overvaluing what we already own or chose (endowment effect); overrating our own conclusions and people we identify with. *Investing:* concentration in things you happen to own; trading too much; believing you can analyze anything. *Check:* is this inside my circle, really, or is my ego enlarging the circle?

13. **Overoptimism Tendency.** Even absent pain to avoid, people are simply too optimistic. *Investing:* hockey-stick projections; underrating base rates of failure. Demosthenes: "what a man wishes, he will believe." *Check:* what does the base rate say, independent of this story?

14. **Deprival-Superreaction Tendency.** Loss aversion plus reaction to *threatened* loss; we react far more to losing (or nearly losing) than to gaining the same amount. *Investing:* selling winners and clinging to losers; doubling down to avoid booking a loss; the "almost had it" pull. *Check:* am I taking risk to avoid a loss rather than to earn a return?

15. **Social-Proof Tendency.** We copy others, especially under uncertainty or stress. *Investing:* the core engine of bubbles and panics; everyone's buying so it must be right. *Check:* would this still look smart if no one else were doing it?

16. **Contrast-Misreaction Tendency.** We judge by contrast, not absolute value, so we get anchored and "boiled like a frog" by gradual change. *Investing:* a price looks cheap only relative to its recent high; small fees ignored next to a large transaction. *Check:* what's the absolute case, ignoring the reference point?

17. **Stress-Influence Tendency.** Stress intensifies other tendencies and can trigger snap (often bad) decisions; extreme stress causes dysfunction. *Investing:* selling at the bottom; decisions made in market panic. *Check:* am I deciding under stress? If so, slow down or pre-commit a rule.

18. **Availability-Misweighing Tendency.** We overweight what's vivid, recent, and easily recalled. *Investing:* recency bias; the dramatic story crowds out the boring base rate; "this time" feels different because it's in front of you. *Check:* am I weighting this because it's *true* or because it's *available*?

19. **Use-It-or-Lose-It Tendency.** Skills atrophy without practice. *Investing:* keep the full latticework in active use; rusty models give bad reads.

20. **Drug-Misinfluence Tendency.** Chemical impairment of judgment. (Mostly a life lesson; relevant to assessing a key person's reliability.)

21. **Senescence-Misinfluence Tendency.** Cognitive decline with age, often unnoticed by the declining person. *Investing:* relevant when a key decision-maker or founder is aging without succession.

22. **Authority-Misinfluence Tendency.** We over-defer to authority figures, even when they're wrong. *Investing:* following a famous investor, a star analyst, or a confident CEO instead of the facts. *Check:* am I deferring to a name rather than to evidence?

23. **Twaddle Tendency.** People emit and consume meaningless chatter that crowds out real work. *Investing:* narrative, jargon, and macro noise substituting for analysis. *Check:* is this signal or twaddle?

24. **Reason-Respecting Tendency.** We comply better and think better when given reasons — but this is exploitable: giving *a* reason (even a hollow one) increases compliance. *Investing:* a plausible-sounding "why" attached to a bad idea lowers your guard. *Check:* is the reason load-bearing, or just present?

25. **Lollapalooza Tendency.** The confluence of several tendencies acting in the same direction, producing extreme, nonlinear outcomes. *Investing:* bubbles (social proof + envy + reward-superresponse + deprival-superreaction + authority + overoptimism) and great compounding businesses (scale + network + brand habit + switching costs). *Check:* after listing the individual tendencies, ask which ones are stacking the same way — that stack is the real story.

## Using the screen on yourself

The hardest application is inward. Before committing capital, explicitly ask which of 5, 11, 12, 13, and 14 — the analyst's classic five (inconsistency-avoidance, denial, self-regard, overoptimism, deprival-superreaction) — are operating on *you* right now. Munger's antidote is to argue the other side better than its proponents can before you're allowed to hold an opinion.
