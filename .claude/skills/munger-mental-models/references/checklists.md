# Munger Checklists & Worksheets

Munger insists on checklists: pilots and surgeons use them because memory under pressure is unreliable, and so is the investor's. Run the relevant checklist explicitly rather than trusting recall.

## Investment Checklist (run before committing capital)

**Understanding & competence**
- [ ] Can I explain how this makes money and what could break it, in plain language? (If no → too-hard pile.)
- [ ] Is this honestly inside my circle of competence, or is self-regard (Tendency 12) enlarging the circle?

**Business quality / moat**
- [ ] What is the durable competitive advantage, specifically? (scale / brand-habit / switching costs / network / regulatory)
- [ ] What is actively trying to destroy that moat, and will it likely survive 10–20 years? (competitive destruction is the default)
- [ ] Is the business riding a wave that will end, or does it have real skill-based advantage?

**Management**
- [ ] Are they able operators *and* honest, rational capital allocators?
- [ ] How are they paid — and does their behavior follow the incentives? (Tendency 1)
- [ ] Any single clear integrity red flag? (If yes → likely disqualifying, regardless of the numbers.)

**Price & risk**
- [ ] Does the price leave a real margin of safety against my being wrong about the inputs?
- [ ] What is the opportunity cost — does this beat my best current alternative, not just cash?
- [ ] Is there any path to *permanent* loss of capital (leverage, fraud, obsolescence, overpayment)? Permanent loss is the only risk that truly matters.

**Psychology audit (on myself)**
- [ ] Am I anchored on my entry price or a past high? (Tendencies 5, 16)
- [ ] Am I in this out of envy or social proof? (Tendencies 8, 15)
- [ ] Am I too optimistic / in denial about a known weak point? (Tendencies 11, 13)
- [ ] Have I argued the *bear* case better than its proponents before allowing myself to buy?

**Decision**
- [ ] Buy / Pass / Too hard — with the single most important reason stated in one sentence.

## Inversion Worksheet

State the goal, then invert it.

1. Goal: e.g., "earn a satisfactory long-term return on this investment."
2. Inverted question: **"What would reliably guarantee a bad outcome here?"**
3. List the failure modes (overpaying; using leverage; thesis creep; ignoring an integrity flag; falling in love with the story; selling in panic; copying the crowd at a top).
4. For each: is the current situation, or my own behavior, exhibiting it right now?
5. Conclusion: avoid the death zones first. "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there." If the idea survives inversion clean, *then* consider the upside.

## The "Too Hard" Pile

A first-class output, not a failure. Put it here when:
- The business model isn't understandable without speculation.
- The outcome depends on macro/political/technological forecasts you can't make.
- The competitive dynamics are genuinely unpredictable.
- You'd have to be at the edge of (or outside) your circle to have a view.

Munger and Buffett pass on the vast majority of opportunities this way. Saying "too hard" preserves the energy and capital for the rare easy call.

## Pre-mortem (decision version of inversion)

Before a major decision: *"Imagine it's two years out and this was a disaster. Write the story of how it happened."* Then check which of those story elements you can see forming today, and which you can defend against now.

## Lollapalooza flag

After any analysis, ask one final question: **are three or more factors (models or psychological tendencies) pushing the same direction and compounding?** If yes, label it a Lollapalooza, size the position and the risk accordingly, and be especially skeptical — the same confluence that builds great compounders also builds bubbles and frauds.
