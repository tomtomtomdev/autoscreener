# The Latticework of Mental Models

Munger's central idea: you must have the big ideas from the major disciplines and use them routinely — *all* of them, not just the few from your own specialty. "To the man with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail." Hang your experience on a latticework of these models so the facts have something to attach to.

Use this file to explain *why* a business wins or loses, or to bring a non-obvious discipline to bear on a problem. Pull the relevant models; don't enumerate all of them.

## Microeconomics — the models that explain competitive outcomes

These are the workhorses for business analysis.

- **Economies of scale.** Bigger can mean lower unit cost, more advertising reach (a vital scale advantage — share of mind), and information/social-proof advantages ("most popular" sells itself). But scale also brings bureaucracy and dysfunction; size is not automatically good.
- **The "surfing" model.** When a new technology or business rides a strong wave (early in an S-curve), even mediocre operators do well; when the wave dies, even good ones struggle. Identify whether returns come from skill or from surfing a wave that will end.
- **Competitive destruction / creative destruction.** The *default* fate of a business is to have its advantages competed away. Durability is the rare exception that must be argued for, not assumed. Most "moats" are temporary.
- **Network effects.** Value rises with the number of users (exchanges, platforms, standards). A genuine network effect is among the most durable moats and a classic Lollapalooza ingredient.
- **Switching costs and habit.** Customers locked in by cost, hassle, integration, or habit (the daily Coke, the entrenched software) produce pricing power and durability.
- **Advantages of being first / building habit early** (the "Coca-Cola" case): brand becomes habit, habit becomes identity, distribution compounds, and several models stack into a Lollapalooza.
- **Cancer-surgery / lollapalooza of disadvantages:** when a business is failing, ask whether the only viable move is to cut everything except a healthy core.

## Mathematics

- **Compound interest.** The most important and most underappreciated force. Small edges and long time horizons dominate; understand it viscerally, not just arithmetically. Frequent activity and frictional costs are compounding's enemies.
- **Basic probability and decision trees.** Think in expected values and probabilities, not point forecasts. Most people never learn elementary Fermat/Pascal probability and pay for it forever.
- **Permutations and combinations / the math of "and."** Many independent things must all go right; multiply the probabilities and the rosy plan looks far less likely.
- **Big numbers and base rates.** Anchor on the population base rate before the specific story. Regression to the mean is real and constant.

## Accounting

- A useful language, but only a tool. **Accounting is the starting point, not the truth.** Know its limits — depreciation assumptions, goodwill, non-GAAP games, EBITDA as "earnings before the bad stuff." Munger: treat reported earnings with suspicion when incentives (Tendency 1) reward inflating them.

## Engineering and physics

- **Margin of safety.** Borrowed from engineering: build the bridge to carry far more than the rated load. In investing, demand a price gap big enough to survive being wrong about the inputs.
- **Backup systems / redundancy.** Don't let a single failure be fatal. Avoid arrangements where one bad event causes permanent ruin (the role leverage usually plays).
- **Breakpoints, critical mass, and equilibrium (physics).** Systems can sit stable then flip suddenly at a threshold (autocatalysis, phase change). Markets and businesses have tipping points; linear extrapolation misses them.

## Biology and evolution

- **Evolution / ecosystems.** Businesses compete in niches like organisms; advantages are relative to the local environment and can vanish when the environment shifts. Survival rewards adaptation, not strength alone.
- **Human nature is evolved and largely fixed.** The psychological tendencies (see the misjudgment file) are evolutionary adaptations misfiring in a modern context — which is exactly why they're so reliable and so exploitable.

## Psychology

The full system is in `psychology-misjudgment.md` (the 25 tendencies). In latticework terms, psychology is the discipline most people *omit* from their analysis and therefore the one that most often blindsides them. Two-track analysis exists to force it back in.

## How to combine them

1. Identify the 2–4 models actually relevant to the situation (usually a microeconomic moat model + compounding + one or two psychology tendencies).
2. Run each independently — what does this lens say?
3. **Check for confluence (Lollapalooza):** are several models pointing the same way and reinforcing each other? That stack, not any single model, is usually the real driver of the outcome.
4. State the conclusion the combined latticework supports, and what would have to be true for it to be wrong.
