# Munger Mental Models — Worldly Wisdom from Poor Charlie's Almanack

Charlie Munger's method is not a valuation formula. It is a way of *thinking*: build a "latticework" of the big ideas from many disciplines, then run any decision through them while actively hunting for the psychological tendencies that corrupt judgment. The aim, in his words, is "to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent."

This skill turns that method into a repeatable process. It is designed to sit on top of quantitative valuation work, not replace it. Value the business with the numbers; use this to decide whether the business is worth owning and whether your own reasoning can be trusted.

## When to use which part

- **Judging a business or stock** → run the **Four Filters** (below), then the **Investment Checklist** in `references/checklists.md`.
- **Pressure-testing a thesis or a decision** → run **Inversion** (below) and the **Bias Screen** from `references/psychology-misjudgment.md`.
- **Reading a person's behavior** (management, a counterparty, yourself) → use the 25 tendencies in `references/psychology-misjudgment.md`.
- **Understanding *why* a business wins or loses** → pull the relevant models from `references/mental-models-latticework.md` (scale, network effects, competitive destruction, critical mass, compounding).
- **Deciding whether to have an opinion at all** → **Circle of Competence** (below). The "too hard" pile is a valid and frequent answer.

Read the reference files when the task calls for that depth. Don't dump all of them; pull what the decision needs.

## The Four Filters (Munger's gate for owning a business)

Before any price discussion, a business must pass all four. Failing one is usually disqualifying; do not average them away.

1. **Can I understand it?** Is the business inside my circle of competence — can I explain how it makes money and what could break it, without hand-waving? If not, it goes on the "too hard" pile. This is a filter, not a failing grade.
2. **Does it have a durable competitive advantage?** A moat that will likely still be there in 10–20 years: brand/habit, low-cost scale, switching costs, network effects, regulatory or geographic lock. Ask what is actively trying to destroy it (competitive destruction is the default; durability is the exception).
3. **Is management able and trustworthy?** Capable operators *and* honest capital allocators who think like owners. Watch incentives (Tendency 1) more than statements. Trust is binary at the extremes — a single clear integrity red flag outweighs a good story.
4. **Is the price sensible, with a margin of safety?** "A great business at a fair price is superior to a fair business at a great price." This is where your valuation skills supply the number; this filter only asks whether that number leaves real room for error.

Output the four filters explicitly as pass / fail / unsure, with one line of reasoning each. If filter 1 fails, say so and stop — don't fake an opinion on something outside competence.

## Two-Track Analysis

Munger analyzes every situation on two tracks at once:

- **Track 1 — Rational factors:** the actual economics, incentives, and interests governing the situation. What do the numbers and the structure say?
- **Track 2 — Psychological factors:** the subconscious influences that are *automatically* producing conclusions — often useful, often wrong. What biases are pushing the participants (and you) toward a particular answer?

Always run both. A thesis that only addresses Track 1 is half-analyzed. The Bias Screen in `references/psychology-misjudgment.md` is the tool for Track 2.

## Inversion — "Invert, always invert"

Many problems are best solved backward. Instead of asking "how do I make this investment succeed?", ask **"what would reliably make this fail, and am I doing any of it?"**

Standard inversion pass:
- List the things that would *guarantee* permanent loss of capital here (overpaying, leverage, fraud, obsolescence, thesis creep, falling in love with the story).
- For each, check whether the current situation or your own behavior is exhibiting it.
- Munger's life heuristic: *"All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there."* Identify the death zones and stay out, rather than optimizing the upside.

Inversion frequently kills a thesis faster and more honestly than building the bull case does.

## Circle of Competence

Competence is defined by its **boundary**, not its size. You don't need a big circle; you need to know exactly where its edge is and to stay inside it. For each judgment, state explicitly: is this inside, at the edge, or outside my circle? "Outside — too hard pile" is one of the most valuable answers available and should appear often. Pretending to competence is itself a misjudgment (Tendency 12, Excessive Self-Regard).

## Lollapalooza Effects

The biggest outcomes — good and bad — come when **multiple models or tendencies push in the same direction at once** and compound. A stock mania is social proof + envy + reward-superresponse + deprival-superreaction + authority, all firing together. A great business can be scale + network effects + brand habit + switching costs reinforcing each other.

When analyzing, after listing individual factors, explicitly ask: *are several of these compounding in the same direction?* If yes, the effect is likely far larger (and the risk far higher) than any single factor suggests. Flag Lollapalooza setups loudly — they are where fortunes are made and lost.

## Munger heuristics to apply throughout

- **Be consistently not stupid.** Avoiding standard errors beats chasing brilliance. The downside of a big mistake dwarfs the upside of a clever one.
- **Opportunity cost is the real filter.** Every option competes against your best available alternative, not against zero. A "good" idea you'd compare favorably to cash may still lose to your second-best holding.
- **Incentives dominate.** When the behavior of a person or company puzzles you, look first at how they are paid. "Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome."
- **Sit on your ass.** Great opportunities are rare; act seldom and decisively, then mostly do nothing. Frequent activity is usually the enemy of returns.
- **A few big bets, deeply understood,** beat wide diversification across things half-understood — *within* the limits of your own risk tolerance and circle.

## Output format for a Munger-style analysis

When asked to analyze an investment or decision, structure the response as:

1. **Circle of competence** — inside / edge / outside, one line.
2. **The Four Filters** — each marked pass / fail / unsure with a one-line reason.
3. **Track 1 (rational)** — the core economics, moat read, and incentive structure.
4. **Track 2 (psychology)** — the bias screen: which tendencies are pushing the participants and the analyst, drawn from the reference file.
5. **Inversion** — the failure modes and whether any are present.
6. **Lollapalooza check** — are multiple forces compounding in one direction?
7. **Verdict** — buy / pass / too hard, with the single most important reason, and the opportunity-cost comparison.

Keep it honest and disconfirmation-seeking. The job is to find the reason *not* to act; if it survives that, it's worth acting on.

## Important framing

This skill reconstructs Munger's publicly described thinking framework for analytical use. Paraphrase his ideas; do not reproduce long verbatim passages from the book. The short quoted aphorisms above are well-known and used as labels for the concepts, not as a substitute for owning the source.
