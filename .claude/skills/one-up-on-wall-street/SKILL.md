# One Up on Wall Street — Peter Lynch Framework

Lynch's thesis: amateurs can beat professionals by paying attention to what they see in everyday life, then doing the homework. The framework is story-first, classification-driven, and rigorous on basic numbers.

## Step 1: The story in two minutes

Lynch's rule: you should be able to explain the investment thesis in two minutes to an eighth-grader. Draft it:
- What the company does
- Why earnings will grow (or hold up)
- The major risks
- What price is being paid for what

If you can't draft a clean two-minute story, you don't understand the investment well enough.

## Step 2: Classify the stock into one of six categories

Each category has different expectations:

1. **Slow growers** — 2–4% growth, mature industries. Buy for dividend, not appreciation. Limited upside; verify dividend safety.

2. **Stalwarts** — 10–12% growth, large, established (think consumer staples). Good defensive holdings; expect 30–50% gains over a few years, not multibaggers. Sell when overvalued, recycle into cheaper stalwarts.

3. **Fast growers** — 20–25%+ growth, can come from any size company. Lynch's favorites for big winners. Verify the growth is real, has runway, and isn't already fully priced.

4. **Cyclicals** — earnings rise and fall with the economic or industry cycle. Time entries to early cycle. Most dangerous to misclassify as stalwarts.

5. **Turnarounds** — broken companies trying to fix themselves. High-risk, high-reward. Verify balance sheet survives, plan is real, progress measurable.

6. **Asset plays** — value lies in undervalued or hidden assets (real estate, cash, subsidiaries, patents, NOLs). Patience required; sometimes a catalyst is needed.

State the classification explicitly and analyze on the terms of that category. Many misjudgments come from analyzing a cyclical as if it were a stalwart, or a fast grower as if growth will last forever.

## Step 3: Lynch's favorite signs

Positive signals he liked:
- Boring or dull name; ugly business
- Spin-offs (also covered separately by Greenblatt)
- Institutions don't own it; analysts don't cover it
- Niche dominance
- People have to keep buying the product (consumables)
- Insider buying
- Company buying back its own shares
- Operates in a no-growth industry but is taking share

Negative signals:
- Hottest stock in hottest industry
- "Whisper stocks" promising the moon
- Diworsification (acquisitions outside the core)
- The "next" anything (next Microsoft, next Amazon)
- Excessive dependence on a single customer

## Step 4: The numbers Lynch actually checks

Lynch is plain-spoken but rigorous. For every stock, check:

- **P/E ratio** vs. the company's history and growth rate
- **PEG ratio = P/E ÷ growth rate**. Below 1 is interesting; below 0.5 is exciting; above 2 is generally too expensive. Use a realistic, defensible growth rate, not management's promise.
- **Earnings growth** — real, sustained, broad-based; not from one-off
- **Inventory growth** vs. sales growth — inventory growing faster than sales is a warning
- **Cash position** — net cash per share matters; subtract from market cap for an adjusted P/E
- **Debt** — manageable, especially for cyclicals and turnarounds
- **Profit margin** — improving or deteriorating; vs. peers
- **Dividend record** — paid through recessions; uninterrupted for years
- **Free cash flow** — Lynch wanted real cash, not just accounting earnings

## Step 5: Know what you own

Lynch's recurring point. Address:
- Have you read the annual report?
- Do you understand how the company actually makes money?
- Could you defend the position to someone after a 30% drawdown?

If any answer is no, finish the homework before recommending.

## Step 6: Why is it cheap (or expensive)?

Specific to category:
- Fast grower — is the market doubting growth? Why?
- Turnaround — what's broken, what's the fix, what's the runway?
- Asset play — what's the hidden asset and who else knows?
- Cyclical — where are we in the cycle relative to the multiple?

## Output format

1. **Two-minute story** — the thesis in plain language.
2. **Category** — which of the six, with reasoning.
3. **Lynch checklist** — favorite signs present, warning signs present.
4. **Numbers** — P/E, PEG, growth, debt, inventory, margins, free cash flow.
5. **Why it's mispriced** — the specific reason.
6. **What would change the verdict** — both directions.
7. **Verdict** — buy / watchlist / pass, with the dispositive factor.

## What to avoid

- Don't analyze without classifying. Wrong category = wrong analysis.
- Don't take management's growth promise as the growth rate for PEG.
- Don't ignore the boring stuff (inventory, debt, margin trend) because the story is exciting.
- Don't buy because you like the product. Lynch said: "Use the product as a starting point for research, not as the research."
- Don't confuse a stalwart that's grown big with a fast grower that still has room.
