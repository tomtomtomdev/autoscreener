# Behavioral Biases — Detection and Countermeasures

The human half of Bernstein's tension. The final movement of *Against the Gods* turns to Daniel
Kahneman and Amos Tversky, whose work showed that people violate the rational-choice models in
**systematic, predictable** ways. This is the catalog to run during step 6 of the framework — auditing
the reasoning itself (the user's, your own, or the market's).

For each bias: what it is, the cue that it's operating, and the countermeasure.

---

## Loss Aversion (Prospect Theory's core)

**What.** Losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good. So people take *irrational
risks to avoid locking in a loss* and *cut winners too early to bank a sure gain*.

**Cue.** "I'll sell once it gets back to what I paid." "I can't sell now, that makes the loss real."
Holding losers and dumping winners. Refusing a clearly favorable gamble because the loss branch looms
large.

**Countermeasure.** Evaluate from where you are *now*, not your purchase price — the market doesn't know
or care what you paid (the sunk-cost trap). Ask: "If I didn't already own this, would I buy it today at
this price?"

---

## Framing Effects

**What.** The same choice yields different decisions depending on how it's worded — "90% survival" vs.
"10% mortality," "5% spoilage" vs. "95% fresh." People are risk-averse for gains and risk-seeking for
losses, so the frame flips behavior.

**Cue.** A decision that feels obvious — then feels different when you restate it. Marketing that
stresses one framing. Choices described only in gains, or only in losses.

**Countermeasure.** Deliberately restate the problem in the opposite frame and in absolute numbers. If
your preference flips, the frame — not the substance — is driving you.

---

## Anchoring

**What.** An initial number contaminates subsequent estimates, even when it's arbitrary and irrelevant.
People adjust away from the anchor but not far enough.

**Cue.** Estimates that cluster suspiciously near a recently mentioned figure — a list price, last
year's number, the first analyst's target, a round number.

**Countermeasure.** Generate your own estimate *before* hearing any reference point. When given an
anchor, build the estimate from independent fundamentals and check how far it sits from the anchor.

---

## Availability Heuristic

**What.** We judge probability by how easily examples come to mind — so vivid, recent, or
emotionally charged events feel far more likely than they are.

**Cue.** Overestimating dramatic risks (plane crashes, shark attacks, the last crash) right after
they're salient; underestimating quiet, statistically larger ones. "Everyone's talking about it, so it
must be likely."

**Countermeasure.** Go to the actual base rate and frequency data (§7 of the toolbox). Ask "how often
does this *really* happen?" rather than "how easily can I picture it?"

---

## Overconfidence

**What.** People are far more certain of their estimates than accuracy warrants; "90% confidence
intervals" contain the truth far less than 90% of the time. Bernstein ties this to the great model-driven
disasters — confident quantification breeding false security.

**Cue.** Narrow ranges, point forecasts stated without error bars, "this can't lose," precise
probabilities attached to genuinely uncertain events.

**Countermeasure.** Widen the interval until it feels uncomfortably wide — then widen it more. Ask "what
would have to be true for me to be wrong, and how would I know?" Track past predictions to calibrate.

---

## Representativeness & Base-Rate Neglect

**What.** Judging probability by how well something matches a stereotype or vivid story, while ignoring
the underlying base rate (the Bayesian prior). The compelling narrative crowds out the boring statistic.

**Cue.** "This founder is *exactly* like the early Amazon team, so it'll be the next Amazon" — with no
weight on how rarely that pans out. A detailed story that feels more probable than a simpler one (the
conjunction fallacy).

**Countermeasure.** Start from the base rate, *then* adjust for the specifics (Bayes, §7). The more
detailed and vivid the story, the more suspicious you should be that it's overriding the prior.

---

## Recency Bias

**What.** Over-weighting the most recent data and assuming the current trend continues. The cousin of
extrapolating extremes (regression to the mean, §6).

**Cue.** "It's gone up three years running, so it'll keep going." Projecting the last quarter, the last
crisis, or the last bull run indefinitely forward.

**Countermeasure.** Pull the long history, not just the recent window. Expect reversion toward the
long-run mean, especially after extremes.

---

## Gambler's Fallacy (and the Hot-Hand)

**What.** Believing independent random events "owe" a correction — "red is due after five blacks." The
inverse error (hot-hand) assumes a streak will continue. Both misread randomness, mis-applying the Law
of Large Numbers (toolbox §4) to small samples.

**Cue.** "It's been down so long it has to bounce." "It's on a roll, it can't lose." Any claim that past
*independent* outcomes change the next one's odds.

**Countermeasure.** Ask whether the trials are truly independent. If they are, the past tells you nothing
about the next draw. If they're *not* (skill, momentum, mean-reversion forces), say so explicitly and
model that — don't smuggle it in as a "feeling."

---

## Hindsight Bias

**What.** After an outcome, it feels as though it was predictable all along ("I knew it"), which corrupts
our memory of how uncertain things actually were and breeds future overconfidence.

**Cue.** "It was obvious the bubble would burst." Post-hoc narratives that make a surprising event sound
inevitable.

**Countermeasure.** Keep a decision journal — record the probabilities and reasoning *before* outcomes.
Judge past decisions by what was knowable at the time, not by how they turned out (good process can have
bad luck, and vice versa).

---

## Using This Catalog

Don't lecture the user with the whole list. Identify the *one or two* biases actually distorting the
decision in front of you, name them plainly, and offer the matching countermeasure. The goal is sharper
thinking, not a psychology seminar. Bernstein's point is not that humans are hopeless — it's that knowing
the predictable errors lets us build processes that guard against them.
