# History and Figures — The Story of Risk

Context and attribution for *Against the Gods*. Read this when the user wants the narrative, the
history, or to cite who originated an idea. The book's arc is a slow climb from a world that saw the
future as fate, to one that learned to measure it — and then a final turn to the limits of that
measurement. Keep attributions accurate; if unsure of a date or detail, say so rather than inventing.

---

## The Arc in One Paragraph

For most of history, the future was the domain of the gods and the fates; people did not believe they
could measure or influence risk. The story of how that changed is the story of the book: it begins with
the absence of even a usable number system, runs through the Renaissance gamblers who first cracked the
odds, the mathematicians who built probability into a science, the statisticians who learned to sample
and to spot regression, the economists who connected risk to value and to human psychology, and the
modern theorists who built portfolio management — and ends with a humbling recognition that no amount of
measurement fully tames an uncertain future, because the measurers are human.

## The Cast (Roughly Chronological)

**The pre-history.** The Greeks had geometry and logic but no probability — partly, Bernstein argues,
because they lacked a number system that made calculation easy and because their worldview left the
future to the gods. The **Hindu-Arabic numeral system** (reaching Europe via Fibonacci's *Liber Abaci*,
1202) was a precondition: you cannot do probability without numbers you can compute with.

**Girolamo Cardano** (1500s) — physician, gambler, and author of an early treatise on games of chance;
worked out odds for dice well before the formal birth of probability.

**Blaise Pascal & Pierre de Fermat** (1654) — their correspondence on how to fairly divide the stakes of
an interrupted game founded **probability theory** and the concept of **expected value**. Pascal also
gave us **Pascal's Wager** — the logic of asymmetric consequences (toolbox §3).

**John Graunt** (1660s) — a London tradesman who analyzed mortality records and effectively invented
**statistical sampling** and the life table, the foundation of actuarial science.

**Jacob Bernoulli** (1713, posthumous) — proved the **Law of Large Numbers**: with enough independent
trials, observed frequency converges to the true probability (toolbox §4). He also wrestled with the gap
between *a priori* odds (a fair die) and *a posteriori* odds (inferred from data).

**Abraham de Moivre** (1730s) — described the **normal distribution** (the bell curve) and the idea that
spread is measured by what we now call standard deviation (toolbox §5).

**Daniel Bernoulli** (1738) — introduced **utility** and **diminishing marginal value**, resolving the
**St. Petersburg Paradox** and explaining risk aversion (toolbox §2).

**Thomas Bayes** (published 1763, posthumous) — **Bayes' theorem**, the mathematics of updating beliefs
as evidence arrives (toolbox §7).

**Carl Friedrich Gauss** (early 1800s) — developed the mathematics of the normal distribution and the
method of least squares; the bell curve is often called the Gaussian distribution.

**Francis Galton** (1880s) — a gifted amateur who discovered **regression to the mean** while studying
heredity (sweet peas, then human height), and developed correlation (toolbox §6).

**Frank Knight** (1921) & **John Maynard Keynes** (1921) — independently drew the crucial line between
**risk** (measurable, probabilities knowable) and **uncertainty** (unmeasurable). Keynes: *"About these
matters there is no scientific basis on which to form any calculable probability whatever. We simply do
not know."* This is the distinction at the heart of the skill's "Core Distinction" section.

**John von Neumann & Oskar Morgenstern** (1944) — *Theory of Games and Economic Behavior* founded
**game theory** and put expected-utility decision-making on rigorous axiomatic footing.

**Harry Markowitz** (1952) — **modern portfolio theory**: risk must be judged at the portfolio level,
through the **correlation** among holdings, not asset by asset. Diversification as the "only free lunch"
(toolbox §8).

**Daniel Kahneman & Amos Tversky** (1970s onward) — **prospect theory** and the systematic catalog of
**cognitive biases** (loss aversion, framing, anchoring, availability, representativeness). They are the
book's final turn: the rational models are violated, predictably, by real human minds (see
`references/behavioral-biases.md`).

## Bernstein's Closing Themes (for accurate paraphrase)

- **The tension never resolves.** The whole book builds toward the recognition that the rational,
  measurable apparatus of probability sits uneasily against the irrational, emotional reality of human
  behavior. Both are real; good risk management honors both.

- **Discontinuity and the limits of the past.** Bernstein is wary of models that assume the future will
  resemble the past, or that risk is always normally distributed. The most dangerous moments come when
  confidence in the model is highest. (The book was published in 1996; the LTCM collapse of 1998 and the
  2008 crisis became textbook illustrations of exactly his warning — useful to mention as later
  vindications, while being clear they postdate the book.)

- **The point of it all.** Measuring risk is what gives humans agency over the future — *"the mastery of
  risk"* is, for Bernstein, the defining achievement of modern civilization. But mastery is not
  conquest. The gods, in the form of irreducible uncertainty, never fully leave the room.
