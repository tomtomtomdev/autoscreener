# The Income Account — Line Items and Graham's Treatment

The income account (income statement / profit-and-loss statement) reports
results over a *period*, in contrast to the balance sheet's snapshot. Graham's
aim in reading it: measure **earning power**, test whether earnings are
**recurring and honestly stated**, and see how safely they cover the company's
**fixed charges**.

## 1. Structure, top to bottom

A conventional income account flows:

```
  Net sales (revenue)
− Cost of goods sold
− Selling, general & administrative expenses
− Depreciation (if shown separately)
= Operating income (operating profit)
± Other (non-operating) income and expense
= Income before interest and taxes
− Interest / fixed charges
= Income before taxes
− Income taxes
= Net income
− Preferred dividends
= Income available to common
```

Graham reads it as a cascade of claims: each layer (operating costs,
depreciation, interest, taxes, preferred dividends) is satisfied before anything
reaches the common stockholder. The further down a security sits, the more it
depends on everything above behaving well.

---

## 2. Operating vs. non-operating income

- **Operating income** comes from the company's actual business—selling its
  products or services. This is the durable, repeatable earning power.
- **Non-operating income** includes interest received, dividends from
  investments, gains on the sale of assets, and other items outside the core
  business.

**Graham insists on separating the two.** A company that reports a good total
net income only because it sold a building or booked a tax windfall has not
demonstrated earning power. Strip non-recurring and non-operating items to find
the income the business actually generates year after year.

---

## 3. Depreciation and depletion

- **Depreciation** spreads the cost of fixed assets over their useful lives;
  **depletion** does the same for natural resources (mines, oil, timber).
- It is a real expense even though no cash leaves in the period—the cash left
  earlier when the asset was bought.
- **Graham's caution: inadequate depreciation inflates earnings.** A company can
  flatter its net income by under-charging depreciation; the cost merely
  reappears later as worn-out plant. Check that the depreciation charge is
  reasonable relative to the property account and to prior years. Conversely,
  unusually heavy depreciation can understate true earnings.

---

## 4. Fixed charges and the senior claims

**Fixed charges** are the obligations that must be paid regardless of how
business goes—principally bond interest, plus other contractual charges (e.g.,
certain lease rentals). They are the dividing line in safety analysis:

- **Times fixed charges earned** = income available for charges ÷ fixed charges.
  This coverage ratio is the central test of bond safety (see `ratios.md` for
  Graham's industry minimums and the total- vs. prior-deductions methods).
- **Preferred dividends** are not legally "fixed charges" (they can be skipped),
  but a sound preferred needs comfortable coverage too. Compute coverage of
  preferred dividends on an *inclusive* basis—after interest, the income must
  still cover preferred dividends with margin.

The principle: the more layers of senior, fixed claims sit above a security, the
thinner the cushion protecting it, and the more a bad year threatens it.

---

## 5. Taxes and net income

- **Income taxes** are deducted after interest (interest is tax-deductible;
  dividends are not). A swing in the effective tax rate can move net income
  without any change in operating performance—note it when comparing years.
- **Net income** is the bottom-line profit after all expenses, charges, and
  taxes. **Net income available to common** subtracts preferred dividends—this
  is what backs the common stock's earnings per share.

---

## 6. Per-share figures

- **Earnings per share (EPS)** = income available to common ÷ common shares.
- Use a representative or average figure across several years where earnings are
  cyclical; a single year (especially a peak or trough) misleads.
- Relate EPS to price (P/E and earnings yield) and to dividends (is the payout
  covered?). Retained earnings—EPS minus dividends per share—should accumulate
  in surplus over time if the company is building value.

---

## 7. Spotting earnings that aren't durable

Graham's quality screen for the income account—apply before trusting any net
income figure:

- **One-time gains** — asset sales, insurance settlements, debt-retirement gains,
  litigation awards. Remove them.
- **Under-depreciation** — earnings propped up by a thin depreciation charge.
  Restate at an adequate rate.
- **Reserve manipulation** — profits smoothed by adding to or releasing reserves.
  Trace changes in reserve accounts.
- **Non-operating income dressed as operating** — investment income or
  intercompany items inflating the operating line.
- **Inventory profits** — gains from rising inventory values rather than
  operations, especially in inflationary periods.
- **Tax-rate swings** — a low tax year flattering net income.

After these adjustments you have **adjusted, recurring earning power**—the only
figure worth capitalizing into a valuation. Always cross-check it against
several years of history; consistency is itself evidence of quality.
