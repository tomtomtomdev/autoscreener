# Financial Ratios — Catalogue, Worked Examples, and Graham's Standards

A ratio relates one statement figure to another so it can be judged. Graham
groups them into three families that mirror the analytic workflow: **financial
position** (liquidity and solvency), **earnings power and safety of charges**,
and **value** (price against the statements). For each, this file gives the
formula, what it tells you, Graham's standard where he sets one, and a worked
example.

## 1. Financial-position ratios

### Working capital
`Current assets − current liabilities`
The operating cushion. Must be comfortably positive; a shrinking or negative
figure signals strain. Graham also uses it as the yardstick for how much
long-term debt is prudent.

### Current ratio
`Current assets ÷ current liabilities`
**Graham's standard: ≥ 2.0 for an industrial company.** Below 2 the margin
between near-term obligations and the assets that cover them is thin. Regulated
utilities and some other steady businesses justify lower ratios because their
revenues are predictable.
*Example:* current assets $6,000,000, current liabilities $2,400,000 →
6,000,000 ÷ 2,400,000 = **2.5** — comfortably above the standard.

### Quick (acid-test) ratio
`(Cash + receivables) ÷ current liabilities`
Excludes inventory because it may not convert to cash quickly or at full value.
**Graham's standard: ≥ 1.0.**
*Example:* cash $1,000,000 + receivables $1,800,000 = $2,800,000; current
liabilities $2,400,000 → **1.17** — passes.

### Cash-asset ratio
`(Cash + marketable securities) ÷ current liabilities`
The most stringent liquidity test. A high current ratio but very low cash ratio
points to an inventory-heavy position—pleasing on paper, less so if goods don't
sell.

### Inventory and receivables turnover
`Sales ÷ inventory` and `Sales ÷ receivables`
Falling turnover (inventory or receivables rising faster than sales) warns of
stale goods or slow collections. Compare across years and to peers.

### Solvency: current assets vs. total liabilities
`Current assets ÷ total liabilities`
The strongest companies carry current assets exceeding **all** liabilities, not
merely current ones. This is also the basis for the net-net value test.

### Capitalization mix
`Bonds ÷ total capital`, `Preferred ÷ total capital`, `Common+surplus ÷ total
capital`
Shows how much of the enterprise is financed by senior claims. Heavy bond or
preferred capital magnifies the volatility of common earnings. Graham prefers
common-heavy structures for industrials; more leverage is tolerable for
utilities with stable revenue.

## 2. Earnings and coverage ratios

### Operating margin
`Operating income ÷ sales` — efficiency of the core business. Judge stability
over years, not a single peak.

### Net margin
`Net income ÷ sales` — bottom-line profitability per dollar of sales.

### Times interest earned (fixed-charge coverage)
`Income available for fixed charges ÷ fixed charges`
The central bond-safety ratio. "Income available for charges" means earnings
**before** deducting interest and taxes (interest is paid out of pre-tax
income). See §3 for the correct calculation method and §4 for the minimums.
*Example:* income before interest and taxes $1,800,000; bond interest $600,000 →
**3.0×** — meets the industrial standard.

### Times preferred dividends earned
`Net income ÷ (interest + preferred dividends)` on an inclusive basis
Because the preferred sits behind the bonds, test it after interest: the income
must cover interest *and* preferred dividends together with margin to spare.

### Return on invested capital
`Net operating income ÷ (long-term debt + equity)`
Measures the productivity of all the capital employed, independent of how it is
financed. Durable, above-average returns are evidence of real earning power.

### Earnings on common equity
`Net income (after preferred) ÷ common equity`
The return to the common stockholder's book investment. Read the trend over a
span of years.

## 3. The two coverage methods

Graham is emphatic that fixed-charge coverage be computed correctly, because the
common wrong method overstates the safety of junior securities.

- **Total-deductions method (correct, "overall"/cumulative):** divide total
  income available by *all* fixed charges combined. To judge a junior bond,
  include the interest on every bond senior to and alongside it.
  *Example:* income $3,000,000; senior bond interest $600,000; junior bond
  interest $400,000. Coverage for the junior bond = 3,000,000 ÷ (600,000 +
  400,000) = **3.0×**.

- **Prior-deductions method (rejected):** credit the senior issue with all
  earnings, then ask how many times the junior charge alone is covered by what's
  left. This flatters the junior issue and can make a weak bond look safe.
  *Same example, wrong way:* (3,000,000 − 600,000) ÷ 400,000 = 6.0× — a
  misleadingly rosy figure.

**Always use the total-deductions method.** A security's safety depends on the
whole burden of charges ahead of and including it, not just its own slice.

## 4. Graham's bond and preferred-stock safety standards

Minimum **average** coverage of fixed charges, measured over a span of years
(not a single good year), by the total-deductions method:

| Type of enterprise | Minimum times fixed charges earned (avg) |
|---|---|
| Public utilities | ~2× (some editions: 1.75× floor) |
| Railroads | ~2.5× |
| Industrial companies | ~3× |

Reasoning behind the spread: utilities and railroads have steadier, more
predictable revenues, so a thinner cushion is acceptable; industrials are more
cyclical and need a larger margin. A bond that meets the minimum only in
prosperity, and falls below it in a recession, is **not** safe—Graham judges by
the worst conditions the company is likely to face, and by the average across a
full cycle.

For preferred stock, apply the same logic one layer down: compute coverage
inclusive of interest plus preferred dividends, and demand a comparable margin.

## 5. Value ratios

### Book value per share
`Common equity ÷ shares outstanding` — accounting net worth behind a share.

### Tangible book value per share
`(Common equity − intangibles) ÷ shares` — Graham's preferred asset-backing
figure; strip goodwill, patents, and trademarks unless they clearly earn their
keep.

### Net current asset value ("net-net")
`(Current assets − total liabilities) ÷ shares`
Counts only liquid assets and subtracts *every* liability. A stock trading below
this figure is backed by working capital alone—Graham's classic deep-value
bargain. (He often looked for a price below two-thirds of net current asset
value as a margin of safety.)
*Example:* current assets $6,000,000; total liabilities $2,800,000; shares
400,000 → (6,000,000 − 2,800,000) ÷ 400,000 = **$8.00** net current asset value
per share. A market price of $5 would be well below it.

### Price/earnings ratio and earnings yield
`Price ÷ EPS` and its inverse `EPS ÷ price`. Judge the multiple against the
stability and growth of earnings, and compare the earnings yield to prevailing
bond yields to gauge relative attractiveness. A high P/E is only justified by
durable, growing earnings; a low P/E on stable earnings with intact tangible
book is a value signal.

## 6. A fully worked mini-analysis

Figures (industrial company):
- Current assets $6,000,000 (of which cash $1,000,000, receivables $1,800,000,
  inventory $3,200,000)
- Current liabilities $2,400,000
- Long-term bonds $400,000 (interest $24,000... use $600,000 interest for the
  coverage illustration below to keep it round); total liabilities $2,800,000
- Common equity $7,200,000; intangibles $1,200,000; shares 400,000
- Sales $12,000,000; operating income $1,800,000; bond interest $600,000;
  pre-tax income $1,200,000; net income $840,000

**Position:**
- Current ratio = 6,000,000 ÷ 2,400,000 = **2.5** ✓ (≥ 2)
- Quick ratio = (1,000,000 + 1,800,000) ÷ 2,400,000 = **1.17** ✓ (≥ 1)
- Current assets vs. total liabilities = 6,000,000 ÷ 2,800,000 = **2.14** ✓
  (current assets exceed all liabilities)

**Earnings & coverage:**
- Operating margin = 1,800,000 ÷ 12,000,000 = **15%**
- Net margin = 840,000 ÷ 12,000,000 = **7%**
- Times interest earned = 1,800,000 ÷ 600,000 = **3.0×** ✓ (meets industrial 3×)

**Value:**
- Tangible book = (7,200,000 − 1,200,000) ÷ 400,000 = **$15.00/share**
- Net current asset value = (6,000,000 − 2,800,000) ÷ 400,000 = **$8.00/share**
- EPS = 840,000 ÷ 400,000 = **$2.10**; at a $21 price, P/E = **10**, earnings
  yield = **10%**

**Verdict (Graham style):** Financial position is sound—liquidity and solvency
both clear the standards. Earnings cover fixed charges at the industrial
minimum, so the bond is adequately (not lavishly) protected; confirm the 3×
holds as a multi-year average, not just this year. Earnings appear to be
operating in nature—verify no one-time items inflate the $840,000. On value, a
P/E of 10 on stable earnings with $15 tangible book is reasonable; the stock is
above net current asset value, so it is a value candidate on earnings rather
than a net-net bargain. Concluding judgment rests on whether the 3× coverage and
7% margin have been *consistent over a span of years*.
