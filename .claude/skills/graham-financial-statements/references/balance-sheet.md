# Balance Sheet — Line Items and Graham's Treatment

The balance sheet shows what a company owns, what it owes, and the owners'
residual interest, at a single instant. Graham's core insight: read it to test
**solvency** (can the company meet obligations?) and **value** (what assets back
the stock?), restating items conservatively before drawing conclusions.

## 1. Assets

### Current assets
Cash and items expected to turn into cash within the ordinary operating cycle
(roughly a year). Listed in order of liquidity:

- **Cash and equivalents** — currency, bank deposits, and short-term marketable
  securities readily salable. The most reliable asset; high cash relative to
  current liabilities is a mark of strength.
- **Marketable securities** — short-term investments. Should be carried at the
  lower of cost or market; verify they are truly liquid, not locked-up holdings.
- **Receivables (notes & accounts receivable)** — amounts owed by customers,
  net of an allowance for doubtful accounts. Watch receivables rising faster
  than sales (loosening credit, collection trouble). Check the allowance is
  adequate.
- **Inventory (merchandise / stock-in-trade)** — raw materials, work in process,
  finished goods. The least certain current asset because its salable value can
  fall below cost. Graham excludes it from the quick ratio for this reason.
  Inventory swelling faster than sales suggests stale or overvalued goods.

### Fixed assets (property account)
Land, buildings, plant, and equipment used in the business, **carried at cost
less accumulated depreciation** (net book value). Graham warns these figures are
accounting conventions, not market values: the property account may be far above
or below what the assets would fetch. Examine whether depreciation has been
charged adequately—understating it inflates both fixed assets and earnings.

### Investments and other assets
Long-term holdings in subsidiaries or other companies, and miscellaneous assets.
Judge whether they are productive and fairly valued.

### Intangible assets
Goodwill, patents, trademarks, franchises, leaseholds, and similar. These have
no physical substance and their balance-sheet figure is often arbitrary—goodwill
in particular may simply record what was overpaid in an acquisition. **Graham's
rule: for conservative analysis, write intangibles down to a nominal value (or
zero) and work with tangible book value**, unless the intangible demonstrably
generates the earnings that justify its carrying amount.

### Prepaid expenses and deferred charges
Costs paid in advance (insurance, rent) or expenses spread over future periods.
Minor for most analyses; treat deferred charges skeptically since they are
expenses parked on the asset side.

## 2. Liabilities

### Current liabilities
Obligations due within the operating cycle: accounts payable, notes payable due
shortly, accrued wages and taxes, and the current portion of long-term debt.
These are the claims the current assets must cover—the heart of the current and
quick ratios.

### Long-term debt
Bonds, debentures, mortgages, and long-term notes due beyond one year. Senior to
the stock. Heavy long-term debt magnifies risk: in a downturn the fixed interest
must still be paid, which can wipe out the common. Always compare long-term debt
to working capital and to equity.

### Contingent and off-balance-sheet obligations
Guarantees, pending litigation, lease commitments. Read the footnotes; a clean
balance sheet can hide obligations disclosed only in notes.

## 3. Capitalization and net worth

The right-hand side below liabilities shows how the enterprise is financed:

- **Bonds** — senior debt capital (also appears under long-term debt).
- **Preferred stock** — equity with a prior claim on dividends and assets ahead
  of the common, usually at a fixed rate. Senior to common but junior to bonds.
- **Common stock** — the residual ownership. Carried at par or stated value,
  which is an accounting figure unrelated to worth.
- **Capital surplus (paid-in surplus)** — amounts paid in by shareholders above
  par value.
- **Earned surplus / retained earnings** — accumulated profits not paid out as
  dividends. Growth in earned surplus over time is a sign of genuine earning
  power being retained in the business.

**Net worth (book value) = total assets − total liabilities** = common + surplus
(+ preferred, depending on whose book value you compute). For the common
stockholder, subtract any preferred claim. **Tangible net worth** further
removes intangibles—Graham's preferred measure of the asset backing per share.

## 4. Reserves

A frequent source of confusion that Graham addresses directly. "Reserve" can
mean three very different things:

1. **Valuation reserves** (e.g., reserve for depreciation, reserve for bad
   debts) — really *deductions from assets*. These belong against the asset they
   offset, not among liabilities.
2. **Liability reserves** (e.g., reserve for taxes, reserve for pensions) —
   genuine estimated obligations. Treat as liabilities.
3. **Surplus reserves / contingency reserves** — earmarked portions of surplus
   with no specific obligation behind them. These are really part of net
   worth—surplus in disguise—and should be added back to equity.

**Always determine which kind a "reserve" is before computing ratios.**
Misclassifying a surplus reserve as a liability understates net worth; treating
a real liability reserve as surplus overstates it.

---

## 5. Restatement habits before computing ratios

- Write intangibles down to nominal value → use **tangible book value**.
- Reclassify each reserve into asset-offset, liability, or surplus.
- Check that depreciation and the bad-debt allowance are adequate; if not, the
  related assets are overstated.
- Mark inventory and receivables for staleness if they outrun sales.
- Pull contingent obligations out of the footnotes into your mental balance
  sheet.

Only after this cleanup are the position and value ratios trustworthy.
