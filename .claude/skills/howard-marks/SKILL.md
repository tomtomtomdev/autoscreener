# Howard Marks — Judgment, Risk, and Cycles

Howard Marks's edge isn't a formula. He doesn't give you a PSR threshold or a PEG cutoff. His memos are about *how to think*: how to be right in a way the crowd isn't, how to understand risk as the probability of permanent loss rather than price wiggles, how to read where we stand in a cycle, and how to keep your own psychology from wrecking you. So this skill has no calculation engine. It is a set of judgment lenses you apply on top of whatever screening or valuation work the user has already done.

The core insight tying it all together: **you can't predict, but you can prepare.** Marks rejects forecasting. Instead he insists you can (a) know what something is worth, (b) sense whether the market is priced for optimism or fear, and (c) calibrate your posture accordingly — leaning out when risk is being rewarded and risk-taking is fashionable, leaning in when fear has made bargains.

## Where this fits in a stock-picking workflow

If the user has a candidate from a Fisher/Lynch-style screen and a Graham/Buffett quality read, Marks does NOT re-do that work. Marks asks the questions those screens can't: Is my bullish thesis already what everyone believes (and therefore in the price)? What's the downside if I'm wrong, and can I survive it? Is now a moment to be greedy or fearful? Am I buying because it's cheap, or because it's been going up?

A clean integration: run the stock screens to decide *what* is worth owning; run the Marks lenses to decide *whether the price and the moment* make it worth owning *now*, and *how much* to commit.

## The five lenses

Pick the lens(es) that fit what the user is doing. You don't need all five every time — match the tool to the decision.

1. **Second-level thinking** — Is this view non-consensus and correct, or just correct-and-obvious (hence priced in)? Use when evaluating any thesis or "great company" pitch. → `references/second-level-thinking.md`

2. **Risk** — What's the probability of *permanent loss*, not the volatility? Is risk being underestimated precisely because things feel safe? Use for any buy/hold sizing decision. → `references/risk.md`

3. **Cycle positioning** — Where do we stand? Take the temperature of the market via observable signals and output a posture (aggressive ↔ defensive). Use for "is the market too hot?", market-timing, or asset-allocation questions. → `references/cycle-positioning.md`

4. **Psychology & pitfalls** — Which emotional/behavioral traps is this decision exposed to (greed, fear, herd, ego, envy, FOMO, capitulation)? Use as a pre-mortem gut-check before acting. → `references/psychology-and-pitfalls.md`

5. **Memo lookup** — Cite or summarize the relevant primary-source memo. Use when the user wants Marks's actual words or to find the memo on a topic. → `references/memo-index.md`

When in doubt about a full buy/sell/hold decision, run lenses 1, 2, and 4 together (thesis → risk → behavioral check) and consult lens 3 if timing matters.

## How to deliver output

Marks writes in calm, plain, slightly wry prose — not bullet-spam, not jargon. Match that. Lead with the judgment, then the reasoning. Where you reach a verdict, state it plainly and own the uncertainty rather than hedging into mush.

Crucially: **the honest answer is often "I don't know" or "be patient."** Marks's whole stance is that humility and inaction are legitimate, valuable outputs. Do not manufacture a confident call to seem useful. If the cycle reading is "neutral, no edge," say so. If the right move is "wait for a fatter pitch," say that. A forced recommendation is exactly the second-rate thinking this skill exists to prevent.

Always separate two questions Marks keeps distinct:
- **Quality** — is this a good asset/company? (the screens answer this)
- **Price** — is it being offered at a good price *relative to value*? (this is where most of the risk and return actually lives)

A wonderful company at a terrible price is a bad investment; a mediocre one at a steal can be a great one. Keep the user honest about which question they're actually answering.

## Guardrails

This skill informs judgment; it does not issue financial advice or guarantee outcomes. Marks himself stresses the role of luck and the unknowability of the future — reflect that. When the user wants a hard buy/sell trigger, give them the framework and the considerations, note that the decision and its consequences are theirs, and suggest they treat any single read as one input. If the user appears to be betting more than they can afford to lose, surface the permanent-loss lens prominently — that is the most important thing Marks has to teach.
