# Memo Index — Primary Sources

Use this to point the user to Marks's actual memos when they want his words, a citation, or the source on a topic. Marks has written memos since 1990; the full archive lives at **oaktreecapital.com/insights/memos**. He publishes several a year, so memos newer than this index certainly exist — when currency matters or the user wants the latest, say so and offer to search the web rather than implying this list is complete or that dates are exact. Treat the years below as approximate where noted.

## The foundational ones (the philosophy)

- **"The Most Important Thing" (2003)** — the memo that became the spine of the book of the same name. The catalogue of "most important things": second-level thinking, value, the price/value relationship, risk, cycles, the pendulum, contrarianism, defense.
- **"You Can't Predict. You Can Prepare." (2001)** — the anti-forecasting thesis and the "I know" vs "I don't know" schools. Core to the cycle lens.
- **"Risk" (2006)** — the fullest statement of risk-as-permanent-loss vs. volatility, and risk being highest when it feels lowest.
- **"Dare to Be Great" (2006) and "Dare to Be Great II" (2014)** — on the necessity and difficulty of being non-consensus; the asymmetry of active management.

## Cycle / "where do we stand" (pair with cycle-positioning.md)

- **"The Race to the Bottom" (2007)** — credit-cycle warning written before the GFC: capital flooding in, terms loosening, the "too much money chasing too few deals" dynamic. A model of reading the temperature near a top.
- **"The Limits to Negativism" (2008)** — written into the crisis; the discipline of not letting skepticism become its own herd, and recognizing when fear has overshot.
- **"Now It's All Bad?" (2008)** and **"The Long View"** — buying when others are despondent.
- **"There They Go Again... Again" (2017)** — a late-cycle caution memo; a good worked example of the temperature gauge applied to a rich market.
- **"Mastering the Market Cycle" (2018, plus the book)** — the systematic treatment of cycles, the pendulum, and calibrating aggressiveness.
- **"Sea Change" (Dec 2022)** — argues a generational regime shift from falling/low rates to a higher-rate world; one of his most-cited later memos. Followed by **"Further Thoughts on Sea Change" (2023)**.

## Psychology, contrarianism, humility (pair with psychology-and-pitfalls.md)

- **"On the Couch" (2016)** — investor psychology and the emotional swings that drive the pendulum.
- **"I Beg to Differ" (2022)** — the discipline of contrarianism done right vs. being different for its own sake.
- **"The Illusion of Knowledge" (2022)** — the dangers of forecasting and false confidence.
- **"bubble.com" (2000)** — written near the dot-com peak; the suspension of disbelief and "this time is different."

## Value, returns, what matters

- **"Something of Value" (2021)** — co-developed with his son Andrew; reconciles value and growth investing, on not dismissing quality/growth as un-"value."
- **"What Really Matters?" (2022)** — long-term focus vs. noise; what actually drives outcomes.
- **"Selling Out" (Jan 2022)** — the underrated cost and difficulty of selling well; why "sell when it's up" is harder and more fraught than it sounds.
- **"Fewer Losers, or More Winners?" (2023)** — defense vs. offense and the two roads to good returns.

## How to use this in a response

When the user asks "what does Marks say about X" or wants a citation, name the most relevant memo(s) and give a one-line gloss, then — if they want the exact text or it's a topic where his view may have evolved in a newer memo — note that the archive is at oaktreecapital.com/insights/memos and offer to pull the current version via web search. Don't quote long passages from memory as if verbatim; paraphrase and attribute, and flag any quote you're not certain is exact.
