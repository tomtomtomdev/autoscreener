# Psychology & Pitfalls — The Pre-Mortem Gut-Check

> "Experience is what you got when you didn't get what you wanted." — Howard Marks

Marks argues that the biggest investing mistakes are not analytical but **emotional and behavioral** — and that controlling your own psychology is the most durable edge, because information and analytical advantages are fleeting but temperament is rare and hard to copy. Use this lens as a pre-mortem: before acting, check which traps the decision is exposed to.

## The negative influences (the forces that make people buy high and sell low)

Run the decision against each. The goal isn't to feel bad — it's to catch a decision that's being driven by emotion wearing the mask of analysis.

- **Greed** — the desire for more, overriding prudence and risk-awareness. The tell: a thesis that's mostly about how much could be made, with the downside waved away.
- **Fear** — overdone risk-aversion that prevents action when action is most warranted (i.e. when prices are low). The tell: "I'll buy when things are clearer" — but by the time it's clear, it's expensive.
- **Dismissal of logic / history / "this time is different"** — the willing suspension of disbelief that lets bubbles inflate. Whenever you hear a reason why old valuation limits no longer apply, raise an eyebrow.
- **Conformity / herd pressure** — the discomfort of being alone pushes people to capitulate to the crowd at exactly the wrong moment. The tell: "everyone's buying this" as a *reason* to buy.
- **Envy** — watching others get rich is more painful than losing money yourself; it drives people into things they don't understand near the top. The tell: a decision motivated by what someone else made, not by the asset's own merits.
- **Ego** — the need to be seen as right / smart / a winner pushes people toward aggressive, concentrated, high-profile bets and makes it hard to admit error. The tell: holding or doubling down to avoid being wrong rather than because the thesis still holds.
- **Capitulation** — the late-stage surrender where a holdout abandons their discipline and joins the madness right before it ends (buys at the top, or sells at the bottom). The most dangerous because it strikes the previously-sensible.

## Contrarianism isn't a slogan

Marks is a contrarian, but he's careful: **doing the opposite of the crowd is not automatically smart.** The crowd is right most of the time during the middle of moves. Contrarianism pays only at the *extremes*, and only when paired with reasoning about *why* the crowd is wrong and value-based conviction to hold through the discomfort.

> "To buy when others are despondently selling and sell when others are euphorically buying takes the greatest courage but provides the greatest profit." — Sir John Templeton, a line Marks endorses

The discipline: it's not "be different," it's "be different, be right, and know why — and be able to withstand looking wrong in the meantime." Being too far ahead of the turn is, in practical terms, the same as being wrong (you can be insolvent before you're vindicated). Hence margin of safety and staying power matter as much as the call.

## Patient opportunism

Marks's positive counterpart to all this: you don't have to swing. "There are no called strikes in investing" (Buffett's image, which Marks uses) — you can wait for the fat pitch. Most of the time the right action is **inaction**: hold, wait, keep dry powder, and let the market serve up a mispricing. The mistake is feeling you must always be doing something. Patient opportunism — waiting, then acting decisively when the odds are lopsided — is the behavioral edge.

## How to apply

When the user is about to act, ask which of the influences above is in the room:
1. **Name the emotion, if any, actually driving this** — is the thesis the reason, or the rationalization? ("You're describing how much you could make and not what could go wrong — that's the greed tell. Walk me through the downside.")
2. **Check for herd / FOMO / envy** — is "everyone's in it" or "I missed the first move" doing the persuading?
3. **Check for ego/sunk-cost** — if this is an add to a loser, is the original thesis still intact, or is this about not being wrong?
4. **Offer inaction as a legitimate option.** Frequently the most valuable output is "you don't have to do anything here — wait for a better pitch."

Deliver this as a calm gut-check, not an accusation. The point is to give the user a moment of self-honesty before committing capital, which is exactly the discipline Marks credits for survival across cycles.
